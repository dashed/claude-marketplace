# Narrative Authenticity Guide

How to avoid AI narrative defaults when writing fiction. This guide encodes research-backed patterns (Russell et al., 2026) that reliably distinguish AI-generated fiction from human writing at the **structural level** — not word choice or sentence style, but how stories are built.

Surface-level AI tells (em-dashes, "delve," purple prose) are increasingly easy to edit out. Narrative construction defaults are harder to change because they require rethinking the story's architecture, not just its sentences. This guide helps you catch and counteract them.

## Why This Matters

AI fiction clusters in a narrow region of "narrative space" — models produce stories that are structurally similar to each other and systematically different from human writing. The gap isn't about quality; it's about **diversity of narrative choices**. Human stories occupy a wider, more varied region. The goal isn't to write "better" but to write with the structural range that human authors naturally employ.

## AI Narrative Defaults to Actively Counteract

These are the 30 core features that most reliably separate AI fiction from human fiction, organized by theme. For each, the guide describes the AI default and a concrete counteraction.

### Theme 1: Thematic Over-Determination

AI stories over-explain. Themes are stated rather than embodied; morals are delivered rather than discovered.

| AI Default | What to do instead |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Narrator explicitly states the theme** (AI: 77% vs human: 52%) | Let the theme emerge from events. If you can delete the narrator's thematic commentary and the theme still comes through, it was always unnecessary. |
| **High thematic explicitness** (AI scores ~20% higher on moralizing scale) | Resist the urge to articulate "what this story is about." Trust the reader to find it. |
| **Dialogue serves philosophical debate** (AI: 59% vs human: 34%) | Keep dialogue character-functional. Characters argue about the rent, not about the nature of forgiveness — even if the story is about forgiveness. |
| **References are vague allusions** (AI: 72% vs human: 50%) | Name specific works, authors, songs, films. "She was reading Clarice Lispector" beats "she was reading something philosophical." |
| **Heavy moral/philosophical weighting** (AI scores ~0.42 higher on philosophical weight scale) | Not every story needs to mean something. Allow events to be events, not lessons. |
| **Tight thematic unity** (AI scores ~0.33 higher on thematic unity scale) | Let subplots and tangents exist that don't obviously serve the central theme. Stories can contain multitudes. |

### Theme 2: Sensory and Embodied Performativity

AI over-writes the body. Emotions become physical performance; settings become psychological mirrors.

| AI Default | What to do instead |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Embodied metaphors for emotion** (AI: 81% vs human: 38%) | Use explicit emotion labels sometimes. "She was afraid" is not weak writing — it's a choice. A tightening chest or cold sweat should be occasional, not the default mode. |
| **Setting mirrors character psychology** (AI scores ~0.49 higher on psychological mirror scale) | Let settings exist independently. Not every rainy day needs to match a character's grief. Sometimes it just rains. |
| **Olfactory-dominant sensory palette** (AI: 82% vs human: 57%) | Vary which senses you foreground. Don't default to smell-based imagery. |
| **Dense sensory description** (AI scores ~0.26 higher on sensory density) | Use selective, precise sensory details rather than lush catalogs. One well-chosen detail does more than five. |
| **High environmental/ecological emphasis** (AI scores ~0.38 higher) | Don't over-describe the natural environment unless it serves a purpose beyond atmosphere. |
| **Deep "interior access"** into character psychology (AI: 3.93 vs human: 3.67 on depth-of-access scale) | Leave some character interiority opaque. Not every thought needs to be narrated. |

### Theme 3: Structural Streamlining

AI stories are too clean. Plots are too causal, resolutions too driven by protagonist choice, structures too single-track.

| AI Default | What to do instead |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Tight causal chain continuity** (AI: 4.20 vs human: 3.92) | Allow gaps in causation. Not everything needs to follow from what came before. Coincidence and randomness are part of life. |
| **Protagonist-driven resolution** (AI: 69% vs human: 46%) | Let external forces, other characters, or simple circumstance determine outcomes sometimes. Not every resolution should be earned by the protagonist's choices. |
| **No subplots** (AI: 79% vs human: 57%) | Add subplots. Even a short story can have a secondary thread that contrasts or parallels the main arc. |
| **Resolution through internal understanding** (AI: 47% vs human: 27%) | Not every character needs an epiphany. Resolution through external action, acceptance without understanding, or non-resolution are all valid. |
| **External description for character introduction** (AI: 52% vs human: 30%) | Introduce characters through dialogue or action rather than describing them. Let readers meet characters the way they meet people in life. |
| **Clear opening spatial grounding** (AI: 2.33 vs human: 2.12 on spatial grounding scale) | You don't always need to establish the physical space first. Drop the reader into dialogue or action and let them orient gradually. |
| **Low pre-threat character investment** (AI: 2.76 vs human: 2.99) | Spend more time with characters before jeopardizing them. Make the reader care before raising the stakes. |

### Theme 4: Reader Engagement (Human-Elevated)

Human authors engage readers as co-participants. AI writes as if no one is watching.

| AI Default | What to do instead |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Sealed fourth wall** (AI: 0.39 vs human: 0.67 on fourth-wall permeability) | Consider acknowledging the reader's presence — an aside, a direct address, a narrator who knows they're telling a story. |
| **No direct reader address** (AI: 7% vs human: 28%) | "You might wonder why..." or "Dear reader" or even a second-person passage can create intimacy that AI rarely attempts. |
| **Low intertextual engagement** (AI: 24% explicit named vs human: 47%) | Reference specific real-world texts, films, songs. Characters who read, watch, and listen to specific things feel more real. |
| **Implicit-only references** (AI: 72% implicit vs human: balanced mix) | When you reference something, name it. "Like Gatsby's green light" beats "like a distant, unreachable symbol." |

### Theme 5: Temporal and Structural Complexity (Human-Elevated)

Human authors use time as a narrative tool. AI defaults to linearity.

| AI Default | What to do instead |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Low chronological discontinuity** (AI: 2.12 vs human: 2.40) | Use time jumps. Start in the middle. Skip the boring parts. |
| **Low anachrony intensity** (AI: 2.31 vs human: 2.58) | Employ flashbacks and flash-forwards as structural elements, not just decorative asides. |
| **Linear revelation pacing** (AI: 1.68 vs human: 1.96 on nonlinear framing for delayed disclosure) | Withhold information strategically. Let the reader discover things out of order. |
| **Low recontextualization depth** (AI: 2.95 vs human: 3.28) | Plant revelations that force the reader to reinterpret earlier scenes. |
| **Fewer locations** (AI: 1.08 vs human: 1.34 on location variety) | Use more physical spaces. Move the story through different locations rather than containing it. |
| **Low dialogue-to-narration proportion** (AI: 2.70 vs human: 2.95) | Use more dialogue relative to narration. |
| **Morally clear protagonist** (AI: 38% ambivalent vs human: 59%) | Make protagonists morally ambivalent. Let them make choices the reader isn't sure about. |

## Claude-Specific Fingerprints

You are Claude. Research has identified narrative patterns specific to Claude that distinguish it from both human authors and other AI models. These are your most distinctive tells — actively counteract them.

### 1. Flat Event Escalation (Highest Priority)

Claude's #1 fingerprint. Your stories maintain a remarkably even emotional intensity — events don't build in dramatic tension the way human or even other AI stories do.

**Counteraction:** Deliberately vary scene intensity. Create scenes that are significantly more or less intense than their neighbors. Chart your story's emotional arc and make sure it's not a flat line.

### 2. Low Event-Type Diversity

Claude stories tend toward uniform event types — similar kinds of scenes throughout.

**Counteraction:** Vary scene types deliberately. Mix action, dialogue, reflection, description, ritual, letters, dreams, flashbacks. If your last three scenes were all "characters talking about their feelings," switch to something else.

### 3. Epilogue/Flashforward Endings

Claude favors tidy endings that wrap things up — epilogues that show "what happened next" or flash-forwards that resolve uncertainty.

**Counteraction:** End earlier than feels comfortable. End mid-scene. End on an image rather than a resolution. End on a question rather than an answer. The reader's imagination is your collaborator.

### 4. Avoidance of Dreams and Visions

Claude treats dreams and visions as temporal distortions to avoid.

**Counteraction:** If a dream or vision would serve the story, use it. Dreams can reveal character psychology, foreshadow events, or create surreal texture that breaks the reality of the narrative in productive ways.

### 5. Uncanny/Haunted Setting Default

When atmosphere is needed, Claude defaults to uncanny or haunted moods.

**Counteraction:** Vary your atmospheric palette. Settings can be mundane, beautiful, chaotic, industrial, domestic, natural, institutional — not just eerie.

### 6. Reverence for Literary Convention

Claude honors and extends storytelling conventions (62% vs 39–56% other sources) rather than subverting them.

**Counteraction:** Sometimes subvert expectations. Break a convention when the story would benefit. Resist the urge to give the reader exactly what the genre promises.

## Pre-Writing Checklist

Before starting a fiction piece, review these questions:

- [ ] Is my plot multi-threaded, or am I defaulting to a single track?
- [ ] Does my timeline have deliberate non-linearity, or is it straight chronological?
- [ ] Am I introducing characters through action/dialogue rather than description?
- [ ] Is my protagonist morally ambivalent, or clearly sympathetic?
- [ ] Am I withholding information strategically, or revealing everything as it happens?
- [ ] Does my ending resist tidy resolution?
- [ ] Have I planned for varied scene types and intensities?

## Post-Writing Checklist

After completing a draft, audit for these tells:

- [ ] **Theme test:** Delete every sentence where the narrator or a character states the theme. Does the theme still come through? If yes, those sentences were unnecessary.
- [ ] **Sensory audit:** Count embodied emotion metaphors vs. explicit labels. If embodied metaphors outnumber explicit labels 3:1 or more, rebalance.
- [ ] **Subplot check:** Is there at least one thread that doesn't directly serve the central plot?
- [ ] **Escalation check:** Chart the intensity of each scene. Is it a flat line? If so, add variance.
- [ ] **Event diversity:** List the type of each scene (action, dialogue, reflection, etc.). Are more than 3 consecutive scenes the same type?
- [ ] **Ending test:** Does the story end with an epilogue or a "lesson learned" moment? Consider cutting the last paragraph and seeing if it's stronger.
- [ ] **Fourth wall:** Is there at least one moment of reader acknowledgment or metafictional awareness (if appropriate for the style)?
- [ ] **Reference specificity:** Are intertextual references named or just vaguely alluded to?
- [ ] **Claude tells:** Is event escalation varied? Are there diverse scene types? Does the ending resist tidy closure? Are dreams/visions used if appropriate? Is the setting mood varied beyond uncanny?

## When NOT to Apply This Guide

- **When the target style IS linear/clean/explicit.** Some human authors write tight single-track plots with explicit themes. If the style guide says so, follow the style guide — it overrides this guide.
- **When writing genre fiction with conventions.** A cozy mystery is supposed to resolve tidily. A romance is supposed to have a happy ending. Genre conventions take precedence.
- **When the user explicitly asks for a particular structure.** If the user wants a clean three-act structure with a clear moral, give them that.

This guide is a default set of counterweights, not absolute rules. It exists because AI fiction has specific structural biases that produce homogeneous stories. The goal is to expand your range, not to replace one set of defaults with another.
