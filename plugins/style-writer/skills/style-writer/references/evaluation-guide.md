# Evaluation Guide

How to self-score writing against a style rubric, diagnose weak dimensions, and improve.

This guide is **style-agnostic** — it works with any rubric in `writing-styles/<style>/evals/style-rubric.md`. The rubric always has 5 dimensions scored 1–5 (total 5–25). Dimension names vary by style, but they fall into predictable categories covered below.

## Quick Evaluation Checklist

Run this 5-point check before detailed rubric scoring. If the answer to any is "no," you already know where to focus.

- [ ] Does this sound right when read aloud? (voice card's Quick Test)
- [ ] Are the do items present and the don't items absent?
- [ ] Does the structure match the style's signature pattern?
- [ ] Is the tone consistent throughout — no lurches between registers?
- [ ] Would someone familiar with the source style recognize this voice?

## How to Self-Score

### Principles

- **Read the rubric's dimension descriptions carefully.** Each dimension has level descriptors 1–5 that describe what that score looks like. Match to those — don't invent your own criteria.
- **Be honest.** A 3 is a solid partial match, not a failure. Inflated scores produce bad feedback loops.
- **Score each dimension independently.** A piece can have excellent tone (5) and weak structure (2). Don't let one dimension bleed into another.
- **When between two levels, choose the lower one.** If you're debating between a 3 and a 4, score it 3. This prevents grade inflation and gives you clearer improvement targets.

### Step-by-Step Scoring Process

**Step 1: Recalibrate your ear.**
Re-read the voice card (`style/voice-card.md`). Pay attention to the "In a Nutshell" summary and the "Quick Test" at the bottom. Get the voice fresh in your mind before evaluating.

**Step 2: Re-read your output with fresh eyes.**
Read the full piece without scoring yet. Just notice: does it feel right? Where does it feel off? Hold those impressions loosely — they'll guide where to look closely.

**Step 3: Score each dimension.**
For each of the 5 rubric dimensions:

1. **Read ALL five level descriptors** (1 through 5) before choosing. Don't stop at the first one that seems close.
2. **Find the descriptor that matches most closely.** Look for the level where most of its criteria are met.
3. **If between two levels, choose the lower one.**
4. **Note 1–2 specific passages** that justify the score. Quote them. Vague justifications like "the tone felt about right" don't help.
5. **Note 1–2 specific improvements** that would raise the score one level. Be concrete: "Add a motivating question before the definition in paragraph 3" not "improve motivation."

**Step 4: Compute the total.**
Sum all 5 dimension scores. Interpret against the rubric's scoring guide:

| Range | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 21–25 | Excellent match — reads as if written by the original author |
| 16–20 | Good match — captures the voice with minor deviations |
| 11–15 | Partial match — some elements present but inconsistent |
| 6–10 | Weak match — occasional echoes of the style |
| 5 | No match — completely different voice |

**Step 5: Identify the weakest dimension.**
The lowest-scoring dimension is where to focus improvement. If two dimensions tie for lowest, prioritize the one that the voice card emphasizes most — that's the one the reader will notice first.

## Score Report Format

Present evaluation results in this format:

```
## Style Evaluation: [Style Name]

| Dimension | Score | Key Evidence |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| [Dim 1]   | X/5   | Brief justification |
| [Dim 2]   | X/5   | Brief justification |
| [Dim 3]   | X/5   | Brief justification |
| [Dim 4]   | X/5   | Brief justification |
| [Dim 5]   | X/5   | Brief justification |
| **Total** | **XX/25** | [Range label] |

### Weakest Dimension: [Name] (X/5)
[What's missing and what would raise it one level]

### Top Improvements
1. [Specific, actionable improvement]
2. [Specific, actionable improvement]
3. [Specific, actionable improvement]
```

## Common Pitfalls by Dimension Type

Rubric dimensions vary by style, but they cluster into five categories. Identify which category each dimension in your rubric falls into, then watch for the corresponding pitfalls.

### Structural Dimensions

*How the writing is organized: section order, pedagogical sequence, argument flow.*

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Getting the sequence right but making it feel mechanical — each section starts and ends the same way | Vary the transitions between structural elements. Use different entry points: a question, an example, an observation. |
| Including all structural elements but in the wrong proportions — too much motivation, not enough formalization | Re-read 2–3 source examples and count the relative length of each structural element. Match those proportions. |
| Missing the return trip — setting up concepts but never reflecting on them or connecting them back | After each major concept, add one sentence that reflects: what did we learn? Why does it matter? What comes next? |

### Tone Dimensions

*How the writing feels: warmth, formality, authority, relationship to the reader.*

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Oscillating between too formal and too casual — academic jargon in one paragraph, slang in the next | Pick 3–4 "anchor phrases" from the voice card and use them as tone calibrators. If a new sentence doesn't fit their register, rewrite it. |
| Warmth that feels performative rather than genuine — excessive exclamation marks, hollow encouragement | Use *structural warmth* (patient scaffolding, careful pacing, acknowledging difficulty) rather than *verbal warmth* ("Don't worry!" "You've got this!"). |
| Losing the author's personality — technically correct tone but flat, generic | Re-read the voice card's "Signature Moves" section. Incorporate 1–2 of those moves per section to inject personality. |

### Craft Dimensions

*Sentence-level quality: rhythm, length variation, voice (active/passive), punctuation patterns.*

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Correct average sentence length but no rhythmic variation — every sentence is 15–20 words | Deliberately alternate: 2–3 longer, clause-rich sentences that build the argument, then one short punchy sentence that lands the point. |
| Using the right transition words but in a formulaic pattern — "First... Second... Third..." | Vary transitions. Don't use the same connector twice in a row. Mix explicit transitions ("We now turn to...") with implicit ones (a question, a short declaration). |
| Over-relying on one punctuation pattern — all semicolons, or all dashes | Check the voice card for the style's characteristic punctuation. If it uses semicolons, parenthetical asides, and em-dashes, make sure all three appear. |

### Vocabulary Dimensions

*Word choice: technical term density, register, definition discipline.*

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Right register but wrong density of technical terms — too many in a paragraph, or too few | Count technical terms per paragraph in your output and in a source example. Adjust to match. |
| Defining terms that don't need definition, or not defining ones that do | Calibrate to the assumed reader level in the voice card. If the voice card addresses "a student who has completed calculus," don't define "function" but do define "homeomorphism." |
| Using precise terms but not the *style's* preferred terms — saying "thus" when the style says "consequently" | Grep the do/don't list and voice card for preferred transition words and connectors. Build a short substitution list. |

### Repertoire Dimensions

*Rhetorical moves: signature phrases, signposting, cultural references, rhetorical questions.*

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Using signature phrases as decoration, not functionally — dropping them in without purpose | Every rhetorical move should serve a function: signposting, emphasis, connection, or humanization. If a phrase doesn't do one of these, cut it. |
| Using too many signature moves at once — reads like parody | Limit to 1–2 signature moves per paragraph. Space them out. The power of a signature phrase comes from its selective use. |
| Missing the style's characteristic rhetorical questions or asides | Check the voice card's "Signature Moves" section. If it lists rhetorical questions as a move, make sure at least one appears per major section. |

## Improvement Strategies by Score Range

### Scores of 1–2: Weak Match

The style hasn't taken hold yet. The problem is usually fundamental: wrong mental model of the voice.

**What to do:**
- Re-read the **full style guide** (`style/full-style-guide.md`), not just the voice card. You need the deep context.
- Find 2–3 passages from source examples and analyze *why* they work — what specific choices create the effect?
- **Rewrite from scratch** rather than editing. Patching a fundamentally off-voice piece produces Frankenstein prose.
- Before rewriting, write a 2-sentence summary of who the author is and how they relate to the reader. Use that as your compass.

### Score of 3: Partial Match

The structure and intent are there, but something is off — usually tone, rhythm, or the gap between knowing the rules and internalizing them.

**What to do:**
- Focus on the **do/don't list** (`style/do-dont.md`) for specific corrections. A score of 3 often means you're doing something on the "don't" list without realizing it.
- **Read your output aloud.** Does it sound like the Quick Test description in the voice card? Where does it diverge?
- Check for **consistency drift** — many pieces start strong in the style and gradually drift toward your default voice. Compare the first and last paragraphs.
- Pick the single weakest dimension and improve just that one. Don't try to fix everything at once.

### Score of 4: Good Match

Close, but missing subtlety. The mechanics are right; the personality needs refinement. This is usually about adaptive judgment — knowing when to vary the pattern.

**What to do:**
- Look at the voice card's "Signature Moves" section. Are you using them, or just using the general tone?
- Check if you're being **too uniform** — a real author varies their approach. A proof might be terse one time and expansive another. A section might start with a question one time and an anecdote another.
- Look for the style's **adaptive patterns** — how does it adjust for difficulty? For tangential material? For particularly important points? Match those adjustments.
- Compare your output to the full style guide's examples. The gap between 4 and 5 is usually visible in direct comparison.

### Score of 5: Excellent Match

Indistinguishable from the source voice. Only achievable through deep internalization of the style, not mechanical application of rules.

**What this means:**
- The writing doesn't just follow the rules — it makes the same *choices* the original author would make in novel situations.
- Signature moves appear at the right moments, not at regular intervals.
- The voice feels natural, not performed.

## Iterative Improvement Workflow

When self-evaluation reveals gaps, follow this cycle:

1. **Score** the output using the full rubric process above.
2. **Identify** the weakest dimension.
3. **Diagnose** using the pitfalls section — which specific pitfall applies?
4. **Apply** the corresponding fix.
5. **Revise** the output, focusing only on the weakest dimension. Don't touch what's already working.
6. **Re-score** the revised output. Confirm the target dimension improved without others regressing.
7. **Repeat** for the next-weakest dimension if the total score is below 16.

**When to stop:** A total score of 16+ (good match) is a reasonable target for most tasks. Push for 21+ only when the user explicitly asks for a very close match or when the piece is high-stakes.

## Narrative Construction Evaluation (Fiction Only)

When evaluating fiction or creative writing, add a **narrative authenticity audit** alongside the standard rubric scoring. This evaluates structural choices that are orthogonal to surface style.

See [narrative-authenticity.md](narrative-authenticity.md) for the full AI-debiasing guide. The evaluation below distills it into a scoring framework.

### Narrative Dimensions (Score 1–5 each)

| Dimension | 1 (AI Default) | 3 (Mixed) | 5 (Distinctively Human) |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Structural Diversity** | Single-track plot, linear timeline, no subplots | Some non-linearity or subplots, but structure is mostly clean | Multi-threaded plot, nonlinear time, subplots that contrast/complicate the main arc |
| **Thematic Subtlety** | Theme stated explicitly by narrator or character, moral delivered at ending | Theme implied but heavy-handed, some showing-not-telling | Theme emerges from events alone, reader does the interpretive work, no moral closure |
| **Emotional Range** | All emotion conveyed through embodied metaphors and sensory density | Mix of embodied and explicit, some environmental mirroring | Varied modes — explicit labels, behavioral cues, selective sensory detail, unnarrated gaps |
| **Character Agency & Morality** | Protagonist clearly sympathetic, resolution through protagonist's epiphany | Some moral ambiguity, mixed agency | Morally ambivalent protagonist, resolution through external forces or non-resolution, no authorial judgment |
| **Event & Temporal Complexity** | Flat escalation, uniform scene types, epilogue ending, no flashbacks | Some variation in intensity and scene type, mostly linear with occasional flashback | Varied escalation, diverse scene types, nonlinear structure, ending resists tidy closure |

### Narrative Score Report

Add this section to the standard style evaluation when the piece is fiction:

```
### Narrative Construction: XX/25

| Dimension | Score | Key Evidence |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Structural Diversity   | X/5 | Brief justification |
| Thematic Subtlety      | X/5 | Brief justification |
| Emotional Range        | X/5 | Brief justification |
| Character Agency       | X/5 | Brief justification |
| Event/Temporal Complexity | X/5 | Brief justification |

### Narrative Authenticity Notes
[Flag any detected AI narrative defaults. Reference specific passages.]
```

### Common Narrative Pitfalls

These are the structural equivalents of the surface-style pitfalls above. They are harder to fix because they require rethinking story architecture, not just revising sentences.

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Every emotion rendered as a physical sensation — no character ever simply "feels sad" | Replace 30–50% of embodied metaphors with explicit labels or behavioral cues. Vary the mode. |
| Story resolves when the protagonist "understands" or "accepts" — internal epiphany as resolution | Try resolving through external action, another character's choice, or non-resolution. |
| The narrator explains what the story means in the final paragraph | Delete the last paragraph. If the story is weaker without it, the problem is earlier — the events aren't carrying the theme. |
| All scenes are the same type (usually characters talking about feelings) | Alternate scene types: action, dialogue, description, reflection, flashback, letter, dream. |
| Timeline is strictly linear from beginning to end | Move one scene out of chronological order and see if the story improves. Often it does. |
| The protagonist is clearly "the good one" | Give the protagonist a choice that the reader can't easily defend. Don't resolve the moral tension. |

## Evaluating Someone Else's Writing

The same process works when evaluating text the user provides (not text you wrote):

- Follow the same scoring steps, but be more generous — a human author may deliberately deviate from a style for good reasons.
- Report scores and evidence the same way.
- Frame suggestions as "to move closer to the [style name] voice" rather than "this is wrong."
- If the text is clearly in a *different* style, say so — don't force-fit a rubric that doesn't apply.
