# Narrative Dimensions: Analysis Guide

This reference provides a framework for analyzing **narrative construction** in fiction and creative writing. While [style-dimensions.md](style-dimensions.md) captures HOW the author writes at the sentence level (surface style), this guide captures HOW the author builds stories at the structural level (narrative construction).

**When to use this guide:** Whenever the source text is fiction, creative nonfiction, or any narrative-driven writing. Surface style and narrative construction are orthogonal — capturing both produces a far more complete and harder-to-fake style profile.

**Research basis:** These dimensions draw on the NarraBench narrative taxonomy (Hamilton et al., 2025) and empirical findings from StoryScope (Russell et al., 2026), which identified 30 core narrative features that reliably distinguish authors through structural choices rather than surface style.

Work through each dimension systematically. Not every dimension will be equally prominent — note when a dimension is neutral or unremarkable, as that is itself a finding.

---

## 1. Plot Architecture

### What It Is

How the author structures the story's backbone: the number of plot threads, their relationship to each other, how tightly events are causally linked, and whether the plot follows a clean arc or a messier, more organic structure.

### What to Look For

- Single-track vs. multi-subplot structure
- How subplots relate to the main theme: parallel, contrasting, independent, or absent
- Causal chain continuity: does each event follow logically from the previous, or are there gaps and coincidences?
- Narrative schema: quest/journey, mystery/investigation, transformation/redemption, siege/ordeal, slice-of-life, trial/test, framed recollection, heist
- How cleanly the plot resolves: tidy wrap-up vs. loose ends vs. deliberate ambiguity

### Analysis Prompts

1. Can you summarize the plot in a single causal chain, or does it require parallel threads?
2. How many subplots exist? Do they reinforce or contrast with the central theme?
3. Are there events that happen by coincidence rather than causation? Is that deliberate?
4. Does the plot follow a recognizable schema (quest, mystery, transformation), or resist categorization?
5. At the end, how many threads are resolved vs. left open?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Structure | single-track, dual-track, multi-threaded, braided, episodic, fragmentary |
| Subplot integration | no subplots, thematically parallel, contrasting, independent, convergent |
| Causal tightness | airtight, mostly continuous, loose, deliberately gapped, coincidence-driven |
| Schema | quest, mystery, transformation, siege, slice-of-life, hybrid, genre-resistant |
| Resolution | fully resolved, mostly resolved, selectively open, deliberately ambiguous |

### Example Observation

> The author favors braided multi-thread plots with 2–3 subplots that contrast thematically with the main arc. Causal chains are loose — characters stumble into situations rather than driving toward them. The primary schema is investigation, but individual chapters read as slice-of-life, creating tension between the forward-driving mystery and the contemplative daily rhythms. Resolution is selective: the central mystery resolves, but character relationships are left in ambiguous states.

## 2. Temporal Structure

### What It Is

How the author handles time: whether the narrative moves forward linearly, uses flashbacks and flash-forwards, employs time jumps, or fragments chronology for effect. Temporal choices are among the most reliably distinctive features of an author's narrative construction.

### What to Look For

- Linear vs. nonlinear progression
- Frequency and function of flashbacks
- Flash-forwards or prolepsis
- Time jumps: how large, how often, how signaled
- Anachrony: how heavily the narrative relies on out-of-order events
- Story duration: hours, days, weeks, years, generations
- Scene duration variation: some scenes compressed, others expanded

### Analysis Prompts

1. If you rearranged events chronologically, how different would the reading experience be?
2. How often do flashbacks appear? Are they brief glimpses or extended scenes?
3. Does the author use time jumps between chapters or within chapters?
4. Is the timeline continuous or fragmented? How does fragmentation serve the story?
5. Are there scenes that take 10 pages for 5 minutes of story time alongside scenes that cover months in a paragraph?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Progression | strictly linear, mostly linear with flashbacks, nonlinear, fragmented, reverse-chronological |
| Flashback frequency | absent, rare, occasional, regular, dominant |
| Time jumps | none, chapter-level, within-chapter, frequent micro-jumps |
| Anachrony intensity | absent, light, moderate, heavy, dominant |
| Duration pacing | compressed, even, expanded, highly variable |

### Example Observation

> The author uses heavily nonlinear structure — the narrative opens at the midpoint of the chronological story, spirals backward through flashbacks triggered by sensory details, and only reaches the "present" in the final third. Time jumps are unmarked (no "Three years earlier" headers); the reader must infer temporal position from context clues. Anachrony is the primary narrative strategy, used to withhold information and force the reader to reconstruct causation. Scene durations vary dramatically: pivotal moments expand to 20+ pages while years pass in single paragraphs.

## 3. Character Introduction

### What It Is

How the author brings characters into the story: through dialogue, through action, through external description, through other characters' reports, or through internal thought. This is a surprisingly distinctive authorial choice.

### What to Look For

- Primary introduction method: in-dialogue, in-action, external description, inner thought, others' reports
- Whether characters are described physically on introduction
- How quickly the author establishes character motivation
- Whether characters are named immediately or withheld
- The ratio of showing (actions, dialogue) to telling (description, summary) in introductions

### Analysis Prompts

1. Pick the 3 most important character introductions. What is the reader's first experience of each?
2. Does the author describe what characters look like? When — immediately or gradually?
3. How many sentences pass before we understand what a new character wants?
4. Are characters named immediately, or does the author withhold names (calling them "the woman," "the stranger")?
5. Does the author use the same introduction pattern for all characters, or does the method signal something about the character's role?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Primary method | in-dialogue, in-action, external description, inner thought, others' reports, environmental (through setting) |
| Physical description | immediate, gradual, absent, selective (only distinctive features) |
| Motivation timing | immediate, deferred, emergent, withheld |
| Naming | immediate, deferred, earned, withheld-then-revealed |
| Show/tell ratio | heavily shown (dialogue/action), balanced, heavily told (description/summary) |

### Example Observation

> Characters are introduced through dialogue — we hear them before we see them. Physical description is selective and delayed: the author mentions one or two distinctive features (a scar, a laugh) pages after the character first speaks, never a catalog of appearance. Motivation is emergent rather than stated; characters reveal what they want through contradictions between what they say and what they do. Names are always given immediately. The approach is consistent across major and minor characters.

## 4. Emotional Conveyance

### What It Is

How the author communicates characters' emotions: through explicit labeling ("she felt afraid"), through embodied physical sensation ("her chest tightened"), through behavioral cues ("she checked the lock three times"), or through environmental mirroring ("rain hammered the windows").

### What to Look For

- Dominant mode: explicit labels, embodied/physical metaphors, behavioral cues, environmental mirroring, or ambiguous
- Whether the mode shifts for different emotional intensities
- Sensory density: how thick the physical sensation language is
- Whether setting mirrors character psychology
- The ratio of named emotions to implied emotions

### Analysis Prompts

1. Find 5 passages where a character experiences strong emotion. How does the author convey it in each?
2. Does the author ever name an emotion directly ("she was angry"), or is it always shown indirectly?
3. How often does the physical environment reflect a character's inner state?
4. Is sensory language dense and lush, or sparse and precise?
5. When emotions are ambiguous, does the author leave them ambiguous or resolve them?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Primary mode | explicit labels, embodied metaphors, behavioral cues, environmental mirror, mixed |
| Sensory density | minimal, selective, moderate, dense, lush/overwhelming |
| Environment-psychology link | absent, occasional, regular, pervasive |
| Ambiguity tolerance | emotions always clarified, sometimes left ambiguous, frequently ambiguous |
| Named vs. implied ratio | mostly named, balanced, mostly implied, never named |

### Example Observation

> The author uses explicit emotion labels more often than expected — characters "feel afraid" or "are furious" rather than manifesting it through body language. Sensory description is selective: a single precise physical detail (cold hands, dry mouth) accompanies the label rather than replacing it. The environment rarely mirrors psychology. This creates a direct, unadorned emotional register that trusts the reader to engage with named emotions rather than decode sensory puzzles.

## 5. Thematic Handling

### What It Is

How explicitly the author articulates themes and morals: whether they are stated outright, embedded in narrative events for the reader to infer, or deliberately left ambiguous. Also covers how thematically unified or diverse the work is.

### What to Look For

- Thematic explicitness: does the narrator or a character state the theme/moral directly?
- Whether dialogue serves philosophical debate or stays character-functional
- Thematic unity: does everything serve one central theme, or do multiple themes coexist?
- Moral/philosophical weighting: how heavily the story foregrounds moral questions
- Whether the ending delivers a "lesson" or leaves the reader to draw conclusions

### Analysis Prompts

1. Can you state the story's theme in one sentence? How much work did YOU do to identify it vs. the text spelling it out?
2. Does any character or the narrator explicitly articulate a moral or lesson?
3. Do characters have philosophical conversations, or is dialogue primarily functional?
4. Does everything in the story connect to one central concern, or are there competing thematic threads?
5. At the ending, does the story tell you what to think about what happened?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Explicitness | stated outright, strongly implied, subtly embedded, deliberately ambiguous |
| Narrator commentary | narrator moralizes, narrator observes without judgment, narrator absent |
| Dialogue function | philosophical debate, character revelation, plot advancement, mixed |
| Thematic unity | monothematic, primary with secondary, multi-thematic, thematically diffuse |
| Moral closure | lesson stated, moral implied, question posed, deliberately unresolved |

### Example Observation

> Themes are subtly embedded — the author never states a moral and the narrator never comments on characters' choices. Dialogue is character-functional: even when characters discuss ideas, the conversations reveal character rather than articulate thesis statements. The work is multi-thematic, with competing concerns (duty vs. desire, memory vs. forgetting) that are never hierarchically organized. The ending poses a question rather than answering one. The reader is trusted to find meaning rather than being told where to look.

## 6. Revelation and Information Management

### What It Is

How the author controls the flow of information to the reader: what is withheld and for how long, when surprises land, how dramatically revelations recontextualize earlier events, and whether the overall pacing is front-loaded or back-loaded.

### What to Look For

- What key information is withheld from the reader
- Revelation pacing: early disclosure, gradual release, or back-loaded surprise
- Whether revelations force reinterpretation of earlier events (recontextualization)
- Suspense vs. dramatic irony: does the reader know more or less than the characters?
- How deeply surprises are planted (foreshadowing depth)

### Analysis Prompts

1. What is the single biggest piece of information withheld from the reader? When is it revealed?
2. After any major revelation, do you want to re-read earlier scenes with new understanding?
3. Does the author favor suspense (reader knows less than characters) or dramatic irony (reader knows more)?
4. How early is foreshadowing planted? Are there clues on page 10 that pay off on page 200?
5. Is the revelation pacing front-loaded (big reveals early, consequences later) or back-loaded (slow build to climactic reveal)?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Pacing | front-loaded, evenly distributed, back-loaded, clustered at turns |
| Recontextualization depth | none, light (adds nuance), moderate (changes interpretation), deep (inverts meaning) |
| Information asymmetry | reader knows less, reader equals characters, reader knows more (dramatic irony) |
| Foreshadowing | absent, subtle, moderate, heavy/deliberate |
| Surprise frequency | rare single reveal, periodic reveals, frequent small surprises |

### Example Observation

> Information management is the author's primary narrative engine. Revelation is heavily back-loaded — the first half establishes a seemingly complete picture, then a third-act reveal forces complete reinterpretation of everything that came before. Foreshadowing is deep and subtle: details that seem incidental on first read become crucial on re-read. The author favors suspense over dramatic irony — the reader discovers information alongside the protagonist. Recontextualization depth is extreme: the climactic reveal doesn't just add information, it inverts the moral valence of the protagonist's earlier choices.

## 7. Reader Engagement and Fourth Wall

### What It Is

The relationship the narrative constructs with its audience: whether it acknowledges the reader's existence, breaks the fourth wall, addresses the audience directly, or maintains the fiction that no one is watching.

### What to Look For

- Direct reader address ("you, dear reader")
- Fourth-wall breaking (acknowledging the story as a story)
- Whether the narrator acknowledges the act of storytelling
- Metafictional elements (characters aware they're in a narrative)
- Audience positioning: co-participant, voyeur, judge, confidant

### Analysis Prompts

1. Does the narrator ever address the reader directly? How often?
2. Is there any moment where the story acknowledges itself as a story?
3. Does the narrator position the reader as someone specific (a jury, a friend, a future historian)?
4. Are there self-aware comments about narrative convention ("as stories like this always go")?
5. Does the story treat the reader as present or absent?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Reader address | never, rare asides, occasional, frequent, constant |
| Fourth-wall status | sealed (no acknowledgment), thin (occasional winks), broken (explicit address), absent (no wall to break) |
| Metafictional awareness | none, light (genre awareness), moderate (narrative self-reference), heavy (fully metafictional) |
| Audience role | invisible observer, confidant, judge, co-conspirator, participant |

### Example Observation

> The narrator frequently addresses the reader as a confidant — "you might wonder why she did this, and I'll tell you" — creating an intimate storytelling frame. The fourth wall is thin: the narrator acknowledges storytelling conventions ("if this were a simpler story, they would have reconciled") without fully breaking into metafiction. The audience is positioned as someone the narrator trusts with the real version of events, as distinct from the version other characters believe. This creates a conspiratorial warmth between narrator and reader.

## 8. Intertextual Strategy

### What It Is

How the author engages with other texts, traditions, and cultural references: whether references are explicit and named, diffuse and allusive, or absent entirely. Also covers pastiche, retelling, and self-referential gestures.

### What to Look For

- Named references to specific books, films, songs, or cultural artifacts
- Diffuse allusions to traditions, genres, or literary movements
- Pastiche or retelling of existing stories
- Self-referential gestures (referencing the author's own prior work)
- Whether references require outside knowledge or are self-contained
- The balance between explicit and implicit intertextuality

### Analysis Prompts

1. List every specific cultural reference you can identify. How many are there per chapter?
2. Are references named ("like something from a Dostoevsky novel") or diffuse (echoing Dostoevsky without naming him)?
3. Does the story retell or reimagine an existing narrative?
4. Would a reader unfamiliar with the references miss something essential, or just miss a bonus layer?
5. Does the author reference high culture, pop culture, both, or neither?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Frequency | absent, rare, occasional, regular, pervasive |
| Explicitness | explicit named, implicit/allusive, balanced mix |
| Type | literary, mythological/religious, pop-cultural, historical, scientific, cross-domain |
| Function | atmospheric, characterizing, thematic deepening, humor, structural |
| Dependency | essential (requires knowledge), enriching (rewards knowledge), decorative |

### Example Observation

> Intertextual references are regular and explicitly named — characters quote poets, discuss films, and argue about novels. References are cross-domain (literary classics alongside pop music alongside scientific concepts) and serve character-revealing functions: what a character reads tells us who they are. The references are enriching but not essential — a reader who hasn't read Borges still follows the plot, but recognizes an additional thematic layer if they have. The author never alludes when they can name.

## 9. Moral Framing and Character Agency

### What It Is

How the author positions characters morally and how much agency characters have over their fates. Covers whether protagonists are morally clear or ambivalent, whether resolutions are driven by character choice or external force, and how the narrative treats moral complexity.

### What to Look For

- Protagonist moral clarity: clearly good, clearly flawed, morally ambivalent, genuinely ambiguous
- Resolution agency: protagonist-driven (character choices determine outcome) vs. externally driven (fate, coincidence, institutions)
- Whether the story judges its characters or presents them without judgment
- Moral complexity: binary good/evil, shades-of-gray, everyone-is-wrong, no moral frame
- Whether "bad" choices are punished narratively

### Analysis Prompts

1. Is the protagonist someone you root for? Is it clear whether you're supposed to?
2. Does the protagonist's fate result from their choices or from circumstances beyond their control?
3. Does the narrative pass moral judgment on character actions, or present them neutrally?
4. Are antagonists given understandable motivations, or are they simply obstacles?
5. Could you argue for the opposite moral reading of the story? If so, is that ambiguity intentional?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Protagonist morality | heroic, flawed-but-sympathetic, morally ambivalent, antiheroic, amoral |
| Resolution agency | protagonist-driven, mixed, externally driven, fate/coincidence, institutional |
| Authorial judgment | judgmental, gently evaluative, neutral/observational, absent |
| Moral complexity | binary, spectrum, everyone-culpable, amoral/no-frame |
| Consequence logic | choices have clear consequences, consequences are disproportionate, no moral causation |

### Example Observation

> The protagonist is morally ambivalent — capable of tenderness and cruelty in the same scene, and the narrative never signals which the reader should weigh more heavily. Resolution is mixed: the protagonist makes meaningful choices, but those choices play out within institutional constraints that limit their impact. The author is neutral-observational — characters are presented without judgment, and the reader must decide how to feel. Antagonists have fully developed motivations that the protagonist sometimes acknowledges as valid. Moral causation is absent: good choices don't guarantee good outcomes.

## 10. Event Escalation and Diversity

### What It Is

How the intensity of events builds (or doesn't) across the narrative, and how varied the types of events are. Some authors build steadily to a crescendo; others maintain a flat intensity with sudden spikes; others deliberately resist escalation.

### What to Look For

- Escalation pattern: steady build, flat with spikes, crescendo-then-quiet, roller-coaster, deliberately flat
- Event-type diversity: action, dialogue, internal reflection, description, ceremony, dream, letter
- Whether the climax is the highest-intensity event or whether intensity peaks earlier
- Pacing of quiet vs. intense scenes
- How the story ends: with a bang (climactic), a whimper (quiet denouement), an epilogue, or mid-scene

### Analysis Prompts

1. Chart the intensity of the five most dramatic scenes. Does intensity build across the story?
2. How many different types of scenes are there? (Action, dialogue, reflection, description, ritual...)
3. Where is the single most intense moment? Is it near the end or somewhere else?
4. How does the author transition between high-intensity and low-intensity scenes?
5. Does the ending match the story's peak intensity, or does it deliberately deflate?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Escalation | steady build, flat with spikes, crescendo, roller-coaster, deliberately flat, inverted (high-to-low) |
| Event diversity | uniform (one type dominates), moderate variety, highly varied, kaleidoscopic |
| Climax placement | final-act, two-thirds, mid-story, distributed (no single climax), anti-climactic |
| Quiet/intense ratio | mostly quiet, balanced, mostly intense, alternating |
| Ending intensity | climactic, quiet denouement, epilogue wrap-up, mid-scene cut, ambiguous |

### Example Observation

> Event escalation follows a roller-coaster pattern — intensity rises and falls multiple times rather than building to a single climax. Event types are highly varied: action sequences alternate with epistolary sections, dream sequences, and extended dialogues. The most intense moment occurs at the two-thirds mark; the final third is a long, quiet aftermath. The ending is a mid-scene cut — the story stops in the middle of an ordinary moment, refusing the closure of either climax or epilogue. The author transitions between intensity levels abruptly, without buffer scenes.

## Using This Guide

### When to Apply

Use narrative dimensions whenever the source text is:
- Fiction (short stories, novels, novellas)
- Creative nonfiction with narrative structure (memoir, literary journalism)
- Screenwriting or dramatic writing
- Any text where the author constructs a narrative arc

Skip narrative dimensions for:
- Technical writing, documentation, or reference material
- Academic prose without narrative structure
- Pure argument or persuasion without story elements

### Integration with Surface Style

Narrative construction and surface style are **orthogonal layers**. A complete fiction style profile should capture both:

- **Surface style** (style-dimensions.md): HOW sentences read — tone, rhythm, vocabulary, punctuation
- **Narrative construction** (this guide): HOW stories are built — plot, time, character, theme

The same surface style can produce very different stories depending on narrative construction choices. Hemingway's spare sentences (surface style) combine with his back-loaded revelation pacing and morally ambivalent protagonists (narrative construction) to create his distinctive effect. Either layer alone would be incomplete.

### During Analysis

1. Work through each narrative dimension in order for your first pass
2. On a second pass, note which dimensions are most distinctive for this author
3. Rank by distinctiveness — the top 4–5 will form the narrative section of the voice card
4. Note interactions between dimensions (e.g., nonlinear time structure often correlates with back-loaded revelation pacing)
5. Compare against known AI defaults (see below) to identify features that make this author's construction distinctively human

### Known AI Narrative Defaults

Research (Russell et al., 2026) has identified narrative construction patterns that AI-generated fiction defaults to. When an author's choices diverge from these defaults, that divergence is especially important to capture:

| Dimension | Typical AI default | Human divergence to watch for |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Plot architecture | Single-track, no subplots, tight causal chains | Multi-threaded, loose causation, subplot integration |
| Temporal structure | Linear, minimal flashbacks | Nonlinear, heavy anachrony, time jumps |
| Character introduction | External description | In-dialogue, in-action, deferred description |
| Emotional conveyance | Embodied metaphors, sensory density | Explicit labels, sparse/selective sensory detail |
| Thematic handling | Explicit, narrator states theme/moral | Implicit, reader infers, no moral closure |
| Revelation pacing | Linear reveal, first-clue-to-grand-reveal | Back-loaded, deep recontextualization |
| Reader engagement | No direct address, sealed fourth wall | Fourth-wall breaking, direct reader address |
| Intertextual strategy | Vague allusions, no named references | Explicit named references, balanced mix |
| Moral framing | Protagonist-driven resolution, clear moral | Ambivalent protagonist, external agency, no judgment |
| Event escalation | Tidy build to epilogue ending | Flat/spiky, mid-scene cuts, anti-climactic endings |

Capturing where the author DEPARTS from these defaults is more valuable than capturing where they conform, because departures are harder to replicate and more distinctive.

### Common Pitfalls

- **Confusing plot summary with narrative analysis.** Saying "the protagonist discovers the truth" is plot summary. Saying "revelation is back-loaded with deep recontextualization" is narrative analysis.
- **Analyzing a single work and generalizing.** If extracting from a single novel, note that these are the construction choices of THIS work — the author may make different choices in other works.
- **Treating all narrative choices as equally distinctive.** Most stories have a protagonist, a conflict, and a resolution. What's distinctive is the author's specific approach to each — the HOW, not the WHAT.
- **Ignoring what's absent.** An author who never uses flashbacks, never breaks the fourth wall, and never names an intertextual reference is making choices. Note meaningful absences.
