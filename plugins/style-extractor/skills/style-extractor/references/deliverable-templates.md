# Deliverable Templates

This reference provides templates for all four style extraction deliverables. Each template includes embedded guidance notes (in HTML comments and bracketed placeholders) explaining what each section should contain and how to fill it.

These templates improve on the base templates in `writing-styles/_template/` by adding:
- Embedded guidance comments for each section
- Minimum content expectations
- Specific instructions for what makes each section effective
- More structured formatting for consistency

When filling these templates, replace all `[bracketed placeholders]` and remove all `<!-- HTML comments -->` from the final output.

---

## Template 1: Full Style Guide

**Target length**: 800-1500 words when filled
**Purpose**: Comprehensive reference document capturing all notable style dimensions
**Audience**: Someone writing extended content in this style

````markdown
# Full Style Guide: [Style Name]

> Source: *[Full Title]* by [Author(s)]

<!-- The overview should capture the essential feel in 2-3 sentences.
     Use a vivid metaphor or comparison (e.g., "like a patient professor at
     the whiteboard"). Mention the dominant tone, the reader relationship,
     and the most distinctive quality. -->

## Overview

[2-3 sentences capturing the overall voice and feel. Answer: if you had to describe this voice to someone who had never read the source, what would you say?]

<!-- For each dimension below, write 2-5 sentences of flowing prose.
     Use specific observations from the source text, not generic descriptions.
     Include characteristic phrases in quotes when they reveal the voice.
     Skip or merge dimensions that are unremarkable for this style. -->

## Tone

[Describe the dominant tone. Is it consistent or does it shift? What is the author's implicit stance toward the subject and reader? Use compound descriptors when a single word is insufficient (e.g., "conversational-academic hybrid").]

## Sentence Structure

- **Average length**: [short (10-15 words) / medium (15-25) / long (25-35)]
- **Variation pattern**: [when and why short vs. long sentences appear]
- **Voice**: [active / passive / mixed — specify when each is used]
- **Rhythm**: [describe the characteristic cadence, e.g., "build-build-punch"]

<!-- Include clause complexity and sentence opening patterns if distinctive. -->

## Paragraph Structure

- **Typical length**: [number of sentences, noting any variation by content type]
- **Density**: [sparse / moderate / dense — how many ideas per paragraph]
- **Topic sentence pattern**: [leading / trailing / embedded / absent]

<!-- Note if paragraph length varies between exposition, examples, and argument. -->

## Vocabulary

- **Level**: [accessible / intermediate / advanced / specialized]
- **Jargon handling**: [defined inline / assumed / glossaried / mixed]
- **Register**: [casual / semi-formal / formal / academic]
- **Diction**: [plain Anglo-Saxon / mixed / elevated Latinate]

<!-- Note any recurring favorite words, deliberate word-choice patterns,
     or register shifts between content types. -->

## Transitions

- **Between sentences**: [list characteristic connectors with examples]
- **Between paragraphs**: [describe bridging patterns]
- **Between sections**: [forward/backward references, bridging strategies]

<!-- Capture any transition phrases that are signature to this voice. -->

## Humor and Personality

- **Level**: [absent / subtle / moderate / frequent]
- **Type**: [dry / warm / sharp / witty / anecdotal / ironic]
- **Function**: [pedagogical / atmospheric / bonding / decorative]

<!-- If humor is absent, say so — that itself characterizes the voice.
     Note whether personality comes through word choice, asides,
     examples, or structural decisions. -->

## Directness

- **Approach**: [state-then-explain / build-then-reveal / mixed]
- **Hedging**: [level and characteristic hedging phrases]
- **Confidence**: [assertive / measured / cautious / tentative]

<!-- Note if directness varies by content type (facts vs. opinions). -->

## Emotional Warmth

- **Reader relationship**: [distant expert / professional guide / collegial peer / supportive mentor / enthusiastic coach]
- **Encouragement**: [absent / implicit / occasional / frequent]
- **Acknowledgment of difficulty**: [how the author handles hard material]

## Explanation Structure

<!-- This is often the most important section. Describe the recurring
     pattern for introducing new concepts as a numbered sequence.
     Give the pattern a name if possible (e.g., "the motivation-definition-example
     sequence"). Note how it adapts to difficulty level. -->

[Describe the recurring pedagogical or argumentative pattern. Use a numbered list for the sequence of moves. Note variations for different difficulty levels or content types.]

## Openings

[How do chapters, sections, and paragraphs typically begin? What do first sentences accomplish? Note any framing devices (epigraphs, questions, anecdotes).]

## Endings

[How do structural units close? Summaries, forward references, exercises, open questions? What emotional note do closings strike?]

## Punctuation Habits

<!-- Only document punctuation that is notable — either conspicuously
     present or conspicuously absent. Skip unremarkable patterns. -->

[List each notable punctuation mark with its frequency and function. Note marks that are conspicuously absent.]

## Common Rhetorical Moves

<!-- Create an inventory of 5-10 recurring moves. For each, give
     the phrase or pattern, its function, and approximate frequency.
     These are the fingerprints of the style. -->

- **"[Phrase]..."**: [Function and when it appears]
- **"[Phrase]..."**: [Function and when it appears]
- **[Pattern name]**: [Description of the recurring argument or structural pattern]

## Things to Avoid

<!-- List 6-10 things that would violate this style. Be specific —
     "don't be boring" is useless; "don't use bullet-point lists where
     flowing prose would serve better" is actionable. -->

- [Specific anti-pattern with brief explanation of why it violates the style]
- [Specific anti-pattern with brief explanation of why it violates the style]

## Examples: Neutral Text Rewritten in This Style

<!-- Provide 2-3 examples. For each:
     1. Write a neutral, plain statement of a concept (2-3 sentences)
     2. Rewrite it in the extracted style, demonstrating multiple
        style dimensions simultaneously (tone, structure, vocabulary,
        rhetorical moves, punctuation habits)
     The rewritten versions should be noticeably longer and richer
     than the neutral originals — showing how the style adds texture. -->

### Original (neutral)
> [A plain, textbook-style statement of a concept. Keep it dry and functional.]

### In this style
> [The same concept rewritten to demonstrate the extracted style. Show the characteristic tone, sentence rhythm, transitions, reader address, and any signature moves.]

---

### Original (neutral)
> [A different plain statement — choose a different type of content (e.g., if the first was definitional, make this procedural or argumentative).]

### In this style
> [Rewritten in the extracted style. Demonstrate different dimensions than the first example.]

---

### Original (neutral)
> [A third plain statement — choose yet another content type.]

### In this style
> [Rewritten in the extracted style. This example should showcase the style's adaptive patterns — how it handles a different kind of content.]
````

## Template 2: Voice Card

**Target length**: ~300 words when filled
**Purpose**: One-page quick reference for getting into the voice fast
**Audience**: Someone about to start writing who needs a quick reminder

````markdown
# Voice Card: [Style Name]

> One-page quick reference for writing in this voice.

<!-- The nutshell should be vivid and immediate — use a metaphor or
     comparison that captures the feel. Someone should be able to read
     just this section and have a usable sense of the voice. -->

## In a Nutshell

[2-3 sentences capturing the essential feel. Use a concrete comparison: "Sounds like [vivid description of the persona]." Include the dominant tone, the reader relationship, and the single most distinctive quality.]

<!-- Key traits should be scannable — one phrase each, no sentences.
     Choose 5-6 traits that best distinguish this voice from a generic
     version of the same genre. -->

## Key Traits

- **Tone**: [one phrase, e.g., "conversational-academic — semi-formal with personality"]
- **Formality**: [one phrase describing the formality level and its limits]
- **Warmth**: [one phrase describing the author-reader temperature]
- **Pacing**: [one phrase describing the rhythm and information flow]
- **Reader address**: [one phrase describing pronoun choices and relationship]
- **[Optional 6th trait]**: [one phrase — add only if there's a distinctive trait not captured above, e.g., "Humor: subtle and pedagogical" or "Precision: every term defined before use"]

<!-- The Pattern should describe the typical explanation or argument
     structure as a numbered sequence. This is the skeleton someone
     follows when writing a new section. -->

## The Pattern

1. [First move — e.g., "Motivate: explain why this matters"]
2. [Second move — e.g., "Build intuition: describe informally"]
3. [Third move — e.g., "Define: state precisely"]
4. [Fourth move — e.g., "Exemplify: work through concrete cases"]
5. [Fifth move — e.g., "Reflect: analyze what was accomplished"]

<!-- Signature moves are the distinctive, recognizable habits — the
     things that make this voice THIS voice rather than a generic one.
     Include characteristic phrases in quotes, structural habits,
     and distinctive formatting choices. Aim for 4-6 items. -->

## Signature Moves

- [Distinctive habit or phrase with brief context, e.g., "'We are now prepared to...' (milestone signpost)"]
- [Distinctive habit with context]
- [Distinctive habit with context]
- [Distinctive habit with context]
- [Optional 5th move]
- [Optional 6th move]

<!-- Never Do This should list 3-5 things that would immediately
     break the voice. These should be specific and testable —
     not "don't be unclear" but "don't use undefined jargon." -->

## Never Do This

- [Specific prohibition — something that would break the voice]
- [Specific prohibition]
- [Specific prohibition]
- [Optional 4th prohibition]
- [Optional 5th prohibition]

<!-- The Quick Test gives a visceral, read-aloud check. Describe
     what the writing should SOUND like — as a persona, a setting,
     or a feeling. This is the final gut-check. -->

## Quick Test

Read your draft aloud. It should sound like [vivid description of the persona and setting — e.g., "a patient professor at a whiteboard who genuinely cares whether you understand" or "a sharp colleague walking you through a code review, direct but never dismissive"].
````

## Template 4: Style Rubric

**Target length**: Structured scoring guide with 5 dimensions
**Purpose**: Evaluate how well a writing sample matches the extracted style
**Audience**: Someone scoring AI-generated or human-written imitations

````markdown
# Style Rubric: [Style Name]

> Score writing samples 1-5 on how well they match this voice.

<!-- Choose 5 scoring dimensions that capture the most important and
     distinctive aspects of this style. The suggested dimensions below
     work well for most styles, but adapt them to what matters most.

     For each dimension:
     - Write a one-sentence description of what the dimension measures
     - Provide clear, specific level descriptors for scores 1-5
     - Level descriptors should be concrete enough that two independent
       raters would give similar scores
     - Score 3 should represent "recognizable but inconsistent"
     - Score 5 should represent "indistinguishable from the source" -->

## Dimensions

### 1. [Structural / Pedagogical Patterns]

<!-- This dimension evaluates whether the writing follows the characteristic
     explanation or argument structure. Name it appropriately for the style
     (e.g., "Pedagogical Structure" for teaching texts, "Argument Architecture"
     for persuasive writing, "Narrative Flow" for storytelling). -->

[One sentence describing what this dimension measures.]

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | [No recognizable structure from the source style. Describe what a complete miss looks like.] |
| 2 | [Some elements present but inconsistent. Describe the partial match.] |
| 3 | [Clear structure present but incomplete. Identify what's typically missing at this level.] |
| 4 | [Strong adherence. Describe what's present and what minor elements might be missing.] |
| 5 | [Exemplary match. Describe what full, natural adherence looks like.] |

### 2. Tone and Warmth

<!-- This dimension evaluates the emotional register, reader relationship,
     and personality. Adapt the descriptors to the specific tone profile
     of the extracted style. -->

Does the writing achieve the characteristic [describe the tone target, e.g., "conversational-academic balance with genuine mentoring warmth"]?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | [Completely wrong tone. Describe the mismatch — e.g., "entirely dry/clinical" or "inappropriately casual."] |
| 2 | [Uneven tone. Some personality but inconsistent.] |
| 3 | [Consistent tone that approximates the target but feels slightly generic.] |
| 4 | [Strong match. The reader feels the right relationship. Specific features present.] |
| 5 | [Indistinguishable from the source. Describe what makes it perfect.] |

### 3. Precision and Definition Discipline

<!-- This dimension evaluates vocabulary handling, technical term management,
     and the balance between precision and accessibility. Adapt for styles
     where terminology isn't relevant — for literary styles, this might become
     "Word Choice and Register" or "Diction and Imagery." -->

[One sentence describing what this dimension measures for this specific style.]

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | [Complete failure — e.g., "terms used without definition" or "vocabulary completely wrong register."] |
| 2 | [Partial match — some terms handled correctly, key gaps remain.] |
| 3 | [Adequate — most vocabulary choices appropriate but lacking the distinctive handling patterns.] |
| 4 | [Strong — vocabulary and term handling match the source style with minor deviations.] |
| 5 | [Perfect — every word choice feels native to the voice.] |

### 4. Sentence Craft and Rhythm

<!-- This dimension evaluates sentence-level writing: length variation,
     rhythmic patterns, punctuation habits, and the mechanical feel
     of the prose. -->

Does the writing exhibit the characteristic sentence-level patterns: [list 2-3 key patterns, e.g., "medium-long build sentences with short emphatic punches, active voice, and parenthetical asides"]?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | [No resemblance — e.g., "monotone sentence length, predominantly passive voice."] |
| 2 | [Some variation but no deliberate rhythm. Key patterns absent or awkward.] |
| 3 | [Reasonable mechanics. Some characteristic patterns appear occasionally.] |
| 4 | [Strong sentence craft. The distinctive rhythm is evident and natural.] |
| 5 | [Masterful. The prose has the exact mechanical feel of the source.] |

### 5. Rhetorical Repertoire

<!-- This dimension evaluates the use of characteristic transitional phrases,
     signposting, rhetorical moves, and persuasive strategies. -->

Does the writing use the characteristic [list 2-3 key rhetorical features, e.g., "transitional phrases, signposting, and meta-commentary"]?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | [No recognizable rhetorical moves from the source style.] |
| 2 | [A few characteristic moves appear but feel forced or misused.] |
| 3 | [Several characteristic moves present and used correctly.] |
| 4 | [Rich repertoire deployed naturally. Most signature moves present.] |
| 5 | [Full repertoire, seamlessly integrated. Moves feel habitual, not performed.] |

## Scoring Guide

- **Total**: Sum of all dimensions (5-25)
- **21-25**: Excellent match — reads as if written by the original author
- **16-20**: Good match — captures the voice with minor deviations
- **11-15**: Partial match — some elements present but inconsistent
- **6-10**: Weak match — occasional echoes of the style
- **5**: No match — completely different voice

<!-- Optional: add style-specific scoring notes here, e.g.,
     "For this style, Dimension 1 (Pedagogical Structure) is the strongest
     signal — a score below 3 on that dimension alone suggests a poor match
     regardless of other scores." -->
````

## Notes on Using These Templates

### Filling the Templates

1. Complete the full style analysis (all 17 dimensions) before filling any template
2. Fill the **Full Style Guide** first — it contains the most detail and informs the others
3. Derive the **Voice Card** by distilling the guide to its most distinctive elements
4. Build the **Do/Don't Checklist** by converting observations into actionable instructions
5. Construct the **Style Rubric** by identifying the 5 dimensions that most distinguish this style

### Quality Checks

- **Full Style Guide**: Does every section contain specific observations, not generic descriptions? Are there quoted phrases from the source? Do the rewritten examples demonstrate multiple dimensions simultaneously?
- **Voice Card**: Could someone write a passable imitation from this card alone? Is every item specific to THIS voice, not just "good writing"?
- **Do/Don't Checklist**: Is every item concrete enough to check while editing? Could you hand this to someone unfamiliar with the source and have them apply it?
- **Style Rubric**: Would two independent raters using this rubric give similar scores for the same sample? Are the level descriptors specific enough to distinguish adjacent scores?

### Adapting Dimensions

The suggested rubric dimensions (Structural Patterns, Tone and Warmth, Precision, Sentence Craft, Rhetorical Repertoire) work for most expository and technical styles. For other genres, consider:

| Genre | Possible Dimension Adaptations |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| Literary fiction | Replace "Precision" with "Imagery and Figurative Language" |
| Journalism | Replace "Pedagogical Structure" with "Narrative Architecture" |
| Marketing/copywriting | Add "Persuasive Momentum" or "Call-to-Action Patterns" |
| Academic papers | Add "Citation Integration" or "Evidence Marshaling" |
| Personal essays | Replace "Precision" with "Vulnerability and Self-Disclosure" |
