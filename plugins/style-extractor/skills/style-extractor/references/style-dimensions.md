# Style Dimensions: Analysis Guide

This reference provides a detailed framework for analyzing each of the 17 style dimensions. For every dimension, you will find a definition, specific features to observe, guiding questions, vocabulary for characterization, and an example of what a finding might look like.

Work through each dimension systematically while reading the source text. Not every dimension will be equally prominent in every style — note when a dimension is unremarkable or neutral, as that is itself a finding.

---

## 1. Tone

### What It Is

The overall emotional register and attitude that pervades the writing. Tone is the author's implicit stance toward the subject and the reader — it colors everything without being stated directly.

### What to Look For

- The feeling you get in the first few paragraphs — clinical? inviting? intense?
- Whether the author positions themselves above, beside, or below the reader
- Consistency of register across different types of content (exposition, examples, asides)
- Shifts in tone at structural boundaries (chapter openings vs. mid-section prose)
- The balance between authority and accessibility

### Analysis Prompts

1. If this text were spoken aloud, what kind of room would it be spoken in, and to whom?
2. Does the tone stay constant or shift between sections, topics, or difficulty levels?
3. Is the authority earned through expertise, institutional position, shared experience, or something else?
4. How would the tone change if you removed all content and left only the connective tissue (transitions, asides, hedges)?
5. Does the author seem to enjoy the subject? How does that show?

### How to Describe It

Use compound descriptors when a single word is insufficient:

| Spectrum | Example Terms |
|----------|---------------|
| Formality | formal, semi-formal, conversational, casual, colloquial |
| Warmth | clinical, detached, neutral, warm, intimate |
| Authority | tentative, collegial, confident, authoritative, commanding |
| Energy | measured, steady, energetic, urgent, breathless |
| Attitude | earnest, playful, ironic, reverent, irreverent, sardonic |

### Example Observation

> The tone is conversational-authoritative: the author writes with quiet confidence and deep familiarity but avoids any hint of condescension. The register is semi-formal — complete sentences and precise language, but with contractions, rhetorical questions, and occasional humor that prevent stiffness. The author sounds like someone who has explained these ideas hundreds of times and still finds them genuinely interesting.

## 2. Sentence Length and Structure

### What It Is

The mechanical architecture of sentences: how long they run, how they vary, what grammatical structures they favor, and what rhythmic patterns emerge from sentence-to-sentence variation.

### What to Look For

- Average sentence length in words (sample 20-30 sentences across different sections)
- The range: shortest vs. longest sentences, and what each type accomplishes
- Active vs. passive voice ratio and when each appears
- Clause complexity: simple, compound, complex, compound-complex
- Rhythmic patterns: does the author alternate lengths deliberately?
- Sentence openings: subject-first? adverbial? participial? varied?
- Use of sentence fragments for effect

### Analysis Prompts

1. Pick a representative paragraph and count words per sentence. What is the range? Is there a pattern?
2. When do short sentences appear — for emphasis, transitions, conclusions, or definitions?
3. How does the author handle multi-clause sentences? Is the main point early or late in the sentence?
4. What percentage of sentences are in active voice vs. passive? When does passive appear?
5. Read three consecutive sentences aloud. Is there a rhythmic cadence?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Length | terse (5-10 words avg), short (10-15), medium (15-25), long (25-35), sprawling (35+) |
| Variation | monotone, low variation, moderate variation, high variation, dramatically varied |
| Rhythm | staccato, steady, flowing, build-and-release, syncopated |
| Complexity | simple and direct, moderately layered, densely subordinated, labyrinthine |
| Voice | active-dominant, mixed, passive-dominant |

### Example Observation

> Sentences average 22 words but range from 4-word emphatic fragments to 45-word multi-clause constructions. The characteristic rhythm is build-build-punch: three medium-to-long sentences accumulate an argument, then a short sentence delivers the conclusion. Active voice dominates in exposition (~85%), with passive reserved for formal definitions and attributed discoveries. Sentence openings vary deliberately — perhaps 40% subject-first, 30% adverbial, 20% transitional phrase, 10% other.

## 3. Paragraph Length and Density

### What It Is

How the author chunks information into paragraphs: the number of sentences, how much content each paragraph carries, and what structural role paragraphs play.

### What to Look For

- Typical number of sentences per paragraph (sample across sections)
- Whether paragraphs vary in length or stay uniform
- Information density: how many distinct ideas per paragraph
- Topic sentence patterns: does the first sentence state the point?
- Whether paragraphs serve distinct structural roles (introduction, example, argument, transition)
- Single-sentence paragraphs: how often and for what purpose

### Analysis Prompts

1. What is the typical paragraph length in expository sections vs. examples vs. arguments?
2. Can you identify the paragraph's purpose from its first sentence alone?
3. How many new ideas or pieces of information does a typical paragraph contain?
4. Are there single-sentence paragraphs? What do they accomplish?
5. Does paragraph length correlate with content difficulty?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Length | short (1-3 sentences), medium (4-6), long (7-10), extended (10+) |
| Density | sparse (one idea, loosely developed), moderate (one idea, fully developed), dense (multiple ideas, tightly packed) |
| Unity | tightly focused, loosely thematic, digressive |
| Topic sentences | leading (point first), trailing (point last), embedded (point in middle), absent |

### Example Observation

> Paragraphs typically run 4-7 sentences in exposition. Each paragraph has a single clear purpose — introduce a concept, work an example, or deliver an argument. Topic sentences almost always lead: the first sentence states or signals the paragraph's focus, and the remaining sentences develop it. Information density is moderate; the author gives each idea room to breathe rather than cramming multiple concepts into one paragraph. Single-sentence paragraphs appear occasionally for transitional signposting.

## 4. Vocabulary Level and Register

### What It Is

The word choices that define the text's accessibility and personality: how specialized the vocabulary is, how technical terms are handled, and whether the diction leans formal or informal, Latinate or Anglo-Saxon.

### What to Look For

- General reading level: could a motivated non-specialist follow this?
- Technical term handling: defined on first use? assumed? glossaried?
- Ratio of common vs. specialized vocabulary
- Latinate vs. Anglo-Saxon word preferences (e.g., "utilize" vs. "use", "commence" vs. "begin")
- Register consistency: does the author mix formal and informal freely?
- Favorite words or phrases that recur noticeably
- Whether the author uses metaphor, and from what domains

### Analysis Prompts

1. Highlight every technical term in a section. How many are defined? How many are assumed?
2. When the author has a choice between a simple word and a complex one, which do they choose?
3. Does the vocabulary shift for different audiences or difficulty levels within the text?
4. Are there recurring pet words or phrases? What do they signal?
5. If jargon is used, is it the jargon of one field or several?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Level | accessible, intermediate, advanced, specialized, technical |
| Register | casual, informal, semi-formal, formal, academic |
| Jargon handling | defined inline, defined in footnotes, assumed, mixed |
| Diction | Anglo-Saxon/plain, mixed, Latinate/elevated |
| Consistency | uniform, mostly consistent with occasional shifts, variable |

### Example Observation

> Vocabulary is accessible with deliberate jargon management: every technical term is defined on first use, often with the term bolded at its point of definition. The register is semi-formal — words like "consequently" and "indeed" appear alongside contractions and colloquial phrases. The author favors plain Anglo-Saxon words for exposition but shifts to Latinate vocabulary for formal definitions, creating a natural distinction between conversational and technical registers.

## 5. Transitions

### What It Is

The connective tissue of the writing: how the author moves from one idea, sentence, paragraph, or section to the next. Transitions reveal how the author conceives of the relationship between ideas.

### What to Look For

- Sentence-level connectors: which conjunctions, adverbs, and phrases appear most?
- Paragraph-level bridges: how does the first sentence of a paragraph connect to the previous one?
- Section-level transitions: how are new sections introduced? Forward/backward references?
- Implicit vs. explicit transitions: does the author rely on logical flow or spell out connections?
- Recurring transition phrases that become part of the voice's signature
- Whether transitions are varied or formulaic

### Analysis Prompts

1. List the first three words of 10 consecutive paragraphs. What patterns emerge?
2. What are the five most common transitional words or phrases?
3. Does the author use forward references ("as we will see") or backward references ("as we noted")?
4. How does the author signal a change in topic vs. a continuation of the same topic?
5. Are there transitions that feel uniquely characteristic of this author?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Explicitness | implicit (juxtaposition), semi-explicit (light connectors), explicit (heavy signposting) |
| Variety | formulaic, somewhat varied, highly varied |
| Style | logical ("therefore"), temporal ("next"), conversational ("now let's"), formal ("consequently") |
| Signposting | absent, light, moderate, heavy |
| Forward/backward references | absent, rare, occasional, frequent |

### Example Observation

> Transitions are highly explicit — the reader always knows where they are in the argument. Sentence-level connectors include "Indeed," "Of course," "Consequently," and "Therefore." Paragraph-level transitions use phrases like "We now turn to..." and "Let's consider..." Section-level signposting is heavy, with both forward references ("as we will see in Chapter 6") and backward references ("as we mentioned earlier"). The transition phrases themselves are part of the voice's identity.

## 6. Humor and Personality

### What It Is

The degree and type of personality that shows through the writing, including humor, wit, personal voice, and idiosyncratic expression. Some styles are deliberately impersonal; that itself is a finding.

### What to Look For

- Presence or absence of humor, and its frequency
- Type of humor: dry wit, warm anecdotes, wordplay, self-deprecation, irony, absurdist
- Whether humor is decorative or functional (does it teach, relieve tension, build rapport?)
- Personal opinions or editorial voice breaking through
- Idiosyncratic expressions or coinages
- Cultural references, allusions, or quotations used for personality

### Analysis Prompts

1. Does the author ever make you smile? What provoked it?
2. Is the humor (if present) consistent throughout or reserved for certain contexts?
3. Does the personality come through word choice, asides, examples, or structural decisions?
4. Would the text lose something important if all humor and personality were stripped out?
5. Is the personality restrained or exuberant?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Level | absent, restrained, subtle, moderate, frequent, pervasive |
| Type | dry, warm, sharp, witty, self-deprecating, ironic, absurdist, anecdotal |
| Function | pedagogical (aids learning), atmospheric (sets mood), bonding (builds rapport), decorative (pure entertainment) |
| Voice | invisible (no author presence), muted, present, distinctive, unmistakable |

### Example Observation

> Humor is subtle but consistent — it appears every few pages, never dominating but always present. The type is warm and literary: cultural references, historical anecdotes, and occasional self-aware observations about the writing process. Humor always serves a pedagogical function — it makes a concept memorable or lightens a dense passage. The author's personality is present but restrained; you sense a real person behind the prose without ever feeling the writing is about them.

## 7. Directness

### What It Is

Whether the author states conclusions first and then supports them, or builds to conclusions through accumulated evidence. Also encompasses hedging, qualification, and the author's comfort with declarative statements.

### What to Look For

- The dominant pattern: state-then-explain or build-then-reveal?
- How much preamble precedes the main point in a typical paragraph or section
- Hedging language: "perhaps," "it seems," "arguably," "one might say"
- Declarative confidence: does the author state things flatly or with qualification?
- Whether directness varies by content type (more direct for facts, less for opinions?)
- Use of rhetorical questions vs. direct assertions

### Analysis Prompts

1. In a typical section, does the main point appear in the first paragraph or the last?
2. How many hedging words appear on a randomly selected page?
3. Does the author ever say "I think" or equivalent? How often?
4. Is there a difference in directness between established facts and the author's interpretations?
5. Does the author telegraph conclusions before delivering them, or let them emerge?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Structure | bottom-line-up-front, state-then-explain, build-then-reveal, circuitous |
| Hedging | unhedged, lightly hedged, moderately hedged, heavily hedged |
| Confidence | assertive, measured, cautious, tentative |
| Preamble | none (dives straight in), brief (one sentence), moderate, extended |

### Example Observation

> The style uses build-then-reveal for new concepts — motivation and context come first, then informal intuition, then the formal statement. But for applying known techniques, it switches to state-then-explain. Hedging is minimal; qualifications like "to be sure" are reserved for genuine caveats rather than false modesty. The author is comfortable making declarative statements and does so with quiet confidence rather than aggressive assertion.

## 8. Emotional Warmth

### What It Is

The quality of the relationship the author constructs with the reader. This goes beyond tone to encompass how the author acknowledges the reader's experience, struggles, and humanity.

### What to Look For

- How the author addresses the reader: as a peer? student? customer? stranger?
- Whether difficulty is acknowledged and how (dismissed, sympathized with, normalized)
- Encouragement: explicit ("you can do this"), implicit (supportive structure), or absent
- Whether the author shares vulnerabilities or struggles of their own
- The emotional temperature of examples and analogies
- Whether the reader is treated as an individual or an abstraction

### Analysis Prompts

1. How would you describe the author-reader relationship in one phrase?
2. Does the author ever acknowledge that the material is hard? How?
3. Is there explicit encouragement, and does it feel genuine or formulaic?
4. Does the author share anything personal — experiences, opinions, struggles?
5. Would a reader feel supported, challenged, or alone after reading a difficult section?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Relationship | distant expert, professional guide, collegial peer, supportive mentor, enthusiastic coach |
| Warmth | cold, cool, neutral, warm, intimate |
| Encouragement | absent, implicit, occasional, frequent, pervasive |
| Vulnerability | none, rare admissions, occasional, openly shared |
| Reader as | abstract audience, generic reader, specific persona, individual human |

### Example Observation

> The author-reader relationship is mentor-to-promising-student. Difficulty is acknowledged honestly ("this section demands careful attention") but framed as surmountable with effort. Encouragement is explicit and sincere, not perfunctory. The author occasionally shares their own experience of grappling with the material, which normalizes struggle. The reader feels accompanied through difficult passages rather than abandoned to figure things out alone.

## 9. Explanation Structure

### What It Is

The recurring pattern the author uses to introduce, develop, and solidify new concepts. This is the pedagogical or argumentative backbone of the text — the sequence of moves that repeats across sections.

### What to Look For

- The order of elements: definition, motivation, example, proof, discussion, exercise
- Whether motivation comes before or after formal statements
- How examples relate to abstractions (before, after, interspersed)
- Whether the author uses a consistent template or varies by topic
- Metacognitive narration: does the author explain their own explanatory choices?
- Scaffolding intensity: does it change with difficulty level?

### Analysis Prompts

1. Pick three sections that each introduce a new concept. What sequence of moves does each follow?
2. Is the sequence consistent enough to name? (e.g., "motivation-definition-example-application")
3. Do examples come before or after the abstraction they illustrate?
4. Does the author ever explain why they structured the explanation a certain way?
5. How does the explanation structure change when the material gets harder?

### How to Describe It

| Pattern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Definition-first | Formal statement, then examples and intuition |
| Motivation-first | Context and why-it-matters, then formal treatment |
| Example-first | Concrete case, then generalization |
| Spiral | Same concept revisited at increasing depth |
| Discovery | Reader is led to derive the concept through guided questions |
| Sandwich | Informal → Formal → Informal restatement |

### Example Observation

> The explanation structure follows a consistent six-step sequence: (1) motivate — why does this matter? (2) build intuition informally, (3) state the formal definition, (4) work through labeled examples, (5) state and prove theorems, (6) reflect on what the proof accomplished. Not every section uses all six steps, but the general flow — motivation before formalism, examples before theorems, reflection after proofs — is highly consistent and forms the pedagogical backbone of the style.

## 10. Opening Patterns

### What It Is

How the author begins structural units — chapters, sections, paragraphs, and individual arguments. Openings set expectations, establish tone, and signal what kind of content follows.

### What to Look For

- Chapter openings: do they start with context? a question? a story? a definition?
- Section openings: bridging from previous content or fresh starts?
- Paragraph openings: topic sentences, transitional phrases, or surprising hooks?
- First-sentence patterns: what do the first sentences consistently accomplish?
- Whether the author uses epigraphs, quotes, or other framing devices
- Variation vs. consistency in opening strategies

### Analysis Prompts

1. Read the first sentence of five different chapters. What do they have in common?
2. Read the first sentence of ten consecutive paragraphs. What patterns emerge?
3. Does the author ever open with a question? An anecdote? A bold claim?
4. How quickly does the author get to the section's main topic?
5. Do openings create a sense of anticipation or calm orientation?

### How to Describe It

| Pattern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Contextualizing | Places the topic within a larger framework before diving in |
| Hooking | Opens with a surprising fact, question, or provocative statement |
| Bridging | Connects explicitly to what came before |
| Definitional | Opens with a formal statement or definition |
| Narrative | Begins with a story, anecdote, or scenario |
| Direct | States the section's purpose immediately |

### Example Observation

> Chapters open with contextualizing paragraphs that place the topic within the broader landscape and connect to the reader's likely prior experience. Section openings use bridging sentences that link to the previous section. Paragraph openings are predominantly topic sentences — the first sentence states the paragraph's purpose. The overall pattern creates a sense of being guided through a carefully planned journey rather than encountering isolated topics.

## 11. Closing Patterns

### What It Is

How the author ends structural units — what the final sentences of paragraphs, sections, and chapters accomplish. Closings shape what the reader takes away and how they transition to the next unit.

### What to Look For

- Section endings: summaries? forward references? exercises? provocative questions?
- Paragraph endings: do they land the point, set up the next paragraph, or trail off?
- Chapter endings: do they wrap up neatly, leave threads open, or look ahead?
- Whether the author provides explicit summaries or trusts the reader to synthesize
- The emotional register of endings: conclusive, reflective, anticipatory, celebratory
- Use of exercises, discussion questions, or calls to action

### Analysis Prompts

1. Read the last sentence of five different sections. What do they have in common?
2. Does the author summarize at the end of sections, or assume the reader followed along?
3. Are there forward references in closings ("in the next section, we will...")?
4. How do paragraph endings relate to the next paragraph's opening?
5. What emotional note do closings typically strike?

### How to Describe It

| Pattern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Summarizing | Restates the key takeaway explicitly |
| Bridging forward | Points to what comes next |
| Reflective | Steps back to observe what was accomplished or what it means |
| Open-ended | Leaves a question or thread for the reader to consider |
| Exercising | Ends with practice problems or prompts |
| Conclusive | Delivers a firm, landing-the-point final statement |
| Circular | Returns to the opening image, question, or theme |

### Example Observation

> Sections end with one of two patterns: either a summarizing observation about the key takeaway, or a forward reference that bridges to the next topic. Paragraph endings consistently land the point — the last sentence delivers the conclusion or implication, rarely trails off. Chapter endings combine summary with anticipation: they acknowledge what was accomplished and hint at what comes next. The emotional register of closings is quietly satisfied — the tone of someone who has finished explaining something well and knows the reader is ready to move on.

## 12. Punctuation Habits

### What It Is

The author's characteristic use of punctuation marks beyond basic periods and commas. Punctuation habits are among the most distinctive and easily replicated aspects of a writing style.

### What to Look For

- Semicolons: frequency and function (connecting related clauses? lists?)
- Em-dashes: frequency and function (interruptions? parenthetical asides? emphasis?)
- Parentheses: frequency and what they contain (clarifications? asides? complete sentences?)
- Colons: frequency and function (introducing lists? definitions? explanations?)
- Italics: what triggers italicization (emphasis? terms? titles? foreign words?)
- Bold: what triggers bolding (defined terms? key concepts? section labels?)
- Exclamation marks: present or absent? how often?
- Ellipses: used for trailing off, omission, or dramatic pause?
- Quotation marks: scare quotes, direct quotes, or terms-under-discussion?

### Analysis Prompts

1. On a random page, count each punctuation mark type. What stands out?
2. Does the author prefer em-dashes or parentheses for asides?
3. How are italics and bold used — decoratively, systematically, or rarely?
4. Are semicolons common, occasional, or absent?
5. Is there any punctuation habit that feels distinctly characteristic of this author?

### How to Describe It

| Mark | Frequency Terms |
|------|----------------|
| Any mark | absent, rare, occasional, moderate, frequent, heavy |

Document each notable mark with its frequency and primary function. Note any marks that are conspicuously absent — an author who never uses semicolons is making a stylistic choice.

### Example Observation

> Parenthetical asides are extremely common — often containing complete sentences that add editorial commentary or gentle clarifications. Semicolons appear frequently, connecting related independent clauses. Colons introduce formal definitions and explanatory expansions. Italics are used heavily for emphasis and for labels. Bold is reserved strictly for newly defined technical terms. Em-dashes are conspicuously rare; the author prefers parentheses for all asides. Exclamation marks are absent.

## 13. Common Rhetorical Moves

### What It Is

The recurring argument patterns, signposting phrases, meta-commentary habits, and persuasive strategies that appear throughout the text. These are the author's go-to tools for building arguments, guiding the reader, and managing the flow of information.

### What to Look For

- Recurring phrases that signal specific rhetorical functions (e.g., "Note that..." signals an important aside)
- Signposting: how the author tells the reader where they are in the argument
- Meta-commentary: does the author comment on their own writing process?
- Argument patterns: analogy, example, authority, elimination, reductio
- How objections or alternative views are handled
- Whether the author uses lists, numbered steps, or other structural devices within prose

### Analysis Prompts

1. What phrases appear at least three times across different sections? What function do they serve?
2. Does the author tell you what they are about to do before doing it?
3. How does the author handle counterarguments or potential reader objections?
4. Are there phrases that feel like this author's verbal signature?
5. Does the author use analogies? From what domains?

### How to Describe It

Create an inventory of recurring moves, each with:
- The phrase or pattern
- Its rhetorical function
- Its approximate frequency

Group by function: signposting moves, logical connectors, reader engagement moves, meta-commentary moves.

### Example Observation

> The text has a rich repertoire of signposting phrases: "We now turn to..." (topic shift), "Indeed,..." (supporting evidence), "To be sure,..." (qualification), "We are now prepared to..." (milestone). Meta-commentary appears regularly — the author explains why a proof is structured a certain way. The dominant argument pattern is example-based: claims are almost always followed by worked examples. Counterarguments are handled through the contrastive pattern: show the wrong approach, explain why it fails, then present the correct one.

## 14. Reader Address

### What It Is

How the author refers to and engages the reader through pronoun choices and modes of address. This dimension shapes the reader's sense of involvement and relationship to the text.

### What to Look For

- Pronoun choices: "we," "you," "one," "the reader," "I," or impersonal constructions
- What each pronoun accomplishes: "we" (inclusion), "you" (direct address), "one" (formality)
- Whether pronouns shift in different contexts (examples vs. theory vs. exercises)
- Direct address: questions posed to the reader, imperatives, second-person instructions
- Whether the reader is positioned as active participant or passive recipient
- Use of inclusive language and assumed shared knowledge

### Analysis Prompts

1. What is the dominant pronoun for referring to the reader? Count occurrences on a sample page.
2. Does the pronoun choice change between exposition, examples, and exercises?
3. Are there moments of direct "you" address? What do they accomplish?
4. Does the author use "I" or "we" to refer to themselves? What does each signal?
5. Is the reader positioned as a collaborator, student, observer, or something else?

### How to Describe It

| Pattern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Inclusive "we" | Author and reader explore together |
| Instructional "you" | Author directs the reader |
| Formal "one" | Impersonal, distanced |
| Authorial "I" | Personal, first-person perspective |
| Authorial "we" | The author (or author team) speaking as a group |
| Impersonal | No direct address; passive constructions |

### Example Observation

> The inclusive "we" dominates — the author and reader are positioned as fellow explorers. "You" appears for direct advice and instructions ("you should try this before reading on"). "I" is absent entirely; the authorial voice is always "we." In formal definitions, the construction shifts to impersonal ("a function is called..."), but the surrounding prose immediately returns to "we." The overall effect is that the reader is a participant, not an audience.

## 15. Use of Examples

### What It Is

How the author deploys examples: their placement relative to abstractions, their format, their level of detail, and their relationship to the concepts they illustrate.

### What to Look For

- Placement: do examples come before or after the abstraction?
- Format: labeled and numbered? inline? extended? minimal?
- Level of detail: step-by-step solutions or summarized results?
- Source: real-world, hypothetical, abstract, historical, from other domains?
- Quantity: how many examples per concept?
- Progression: do examples increase in difficulty?
- Whether examples are referred back to later

### Analysis Prompts

1. For a typical new concept, how many examples follow the definition?
2. Are examples worked step-by-step or presented as fait accompli?
3. Do examples ever precede the formal concept they illustrate?
4. Are examples drawn from the real world, the abstract world, or both?
5. Does the author comment on examples after presenting them (meta-analysis)?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Placement | pre-abstraction, post-abstraction, interspersed, sandwiched |
| Detail | minimal (result only), moderate (key steps), detailed (every step), exhaustive |
| Format | inline, labeled/numbered, boxed/highlighted, extended narrative |
| Source | abstract/constructed, real-world, historical, cross-domain |
| Quantity | sparse (0-1 per concept), moderate (2-3), abundant (4+) |
| Progression | uniform difficulty, graduated, alternating simple/complex |

### Example Observation

> Examples always follow the formal definition, never precede it. They are labeled and numbered (Example 3.2) with detailed step-by-step solutions that narrate the reasoning process. Typically 2-4 examples follow each major definition, progressing from straightforward to challenging. The author frequently comments on examples after presenting them, drawing out what the example demonstrates about the general concept. Examples are abstract rather than real-world, but they are always concrete enough to be immediately graspable.

## 16. Cultural and Historical References

### What It Is

How and whether the author incorporates references to history, culture, literature, biography, current events, or other domains outside the text's primary subject matter.

### What to Look For

- Frequency: how often do references appear?
- Types: literary, historical, biographical, scientific, pop culture, other
- Function: humanizing the subject, establishing authority, decorating, providing analogy
- Integration: woven into the text or clearly set apart (sidebars, footnotes, epigraphs)?
- Depth: passing mention or developed anecdote?
- Whether references assume cultural familiarity or are self-contained

### Analysis Prompts

1. List every cultural or historical reference in a chapter. How many are there?
2. What function does each reference serve — could the passage work without it?
3. Are references drawn from one domain (e.g., all literary) or diverse?
4. Does the author provide enough context for a reader unfamiliar with the reference?
5. Do references add to the text's personality, or are they perfunctory?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Frequency | absent, rare, occasional, regular, pervasive |
| Function | authoritative (ethos), humanizing, illustrative (analogy), decorative, atmospheric |
| Type | literary, historical, biographical, scientific, pop-cultural, mixed |
| Integration | woven in, set apart (sidebar/footnote), epigraphic |
| Depth | passing mention, brief context, developed anecdote, extended discussion |

### Example Observation

> Historical and biographical references appear regularly — perhaps once per section. They serve a humanizing function, connecting abstract concepts to the people who developed them. The author provides enough context that no prior knowledge is required. References are woven naturally into the prose rather than set apart in sidebars. The range is broad: literary quotations, biographical anecdotes about scientists, and occasional pop-culture allusions. Each reference earns its place by making the material more engaging or memorable.

## 17. Adaptive Patterns

### What It Is

How the style modulates itself in response to changes in content type, difficulty level, audience, or mode (exposition vs. proof vs. narrative vs. exercise). A style that never adapts is monotone; a style that adapts too much lacks coherence.

### What to Look For

- Does the writing shift register when difficulty increases?
- Are there distinct modes (narrative, exposition, formal, conversational) and clear signals between them?
- Does scaffolding intensity scale with complexity?
- Do sentence length, vocabulary, and warmth change for different content types?
- How does the author handle transitions between modes?
- Is there a "base" mode that the author returns to?

### Analysis Prompts

1. Compare a passage explaining something easy to one explaining something hard. What changed?
2. Does the author shift between narrative and expository modes? What triggers the shift?
3. Is the writing more or less formal in different sections? What governs the shift?
4. Does the author increase scaffolding (more examples, more explanation) for harder material?
5. Is there a recognizable "home base" register that the author returns to?

### How to Describe It

| Aspect | Vocabulary |
|--------|-----------|
| Adaptiveness | rigid (one mode), slightly adaptive, moderately adaptive, highly adaptive |
| Modes | expository, narrative, formal/technical, conversational, instructional, reflective |
| Scaling | fixed scaffolding, difficulty-scaled, audience-scaled, context-dependent |
| Coherence | disjointed, loosely connected, cohesive, seamlessly integrated |
| Triggers | difficulty level, content type, proximity to formal material, section type |

### Example Observation

> The style is highly adaptive but maintains a coherent identity across modes. For straightforward material, the sequence is streamlined: definition, examples, result. For difficult material, extensive scaffolding appears: informal exploration of proof strategy, then the formal proof, then reflective analysis. The "home base" register is conversational-academic; the author shifts toward greater formality for definitions and proofs, and toward greater warmth and informality when acknowledging difficulty or encouraging the reader. Mode transitions are signaled explicitly ("Let's step back and think about what we're trying to do here").

## Using This Guide

### During Analysis

1. Work through each dimension in order for your first pass
2. On a second pass, note which dimensions are most distinctive for this particular style
3. Rank the dimensions by distinctiveness — the top 5-7 will form the core of the voice card
4. Note interactions between dimensions (e.g., humor level may correlate with emotional warmth)

### Common Pitfalls

- **Describing what you expect rather than what you observe**: Let the text speak for itself
- **Treating every dimension as equally important**: Some styles are defined by 4-5 dominant dimensions; the rest are neutral
- **Confusing genre conventions with author style**: Separate what's standard for the genre from what's distinctive about this author
- **Analyzing from a single sample**: Check that your observations hold across multiple sections and content types
- **Over-specifying the unremarkable**: If a dimension is average or neutral, say so briefly and move on
