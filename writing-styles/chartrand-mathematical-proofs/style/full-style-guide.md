# Full Style Guide: Chartrand Mathematical Proofs

> Source: *Mathematical Proofs: A Transition to Advanced Mathematics* (4th ed.) by Gary Chartrand, Albert D. Polimeni, and Ping Zhang

## Overview

This voice belongs to a patient, experienced mathematics professor standing at the whiteboard — authoritative but approachable, rigorous but warm. The writing uses an inclusive "we" that walks alongside the reader through mathematical territory, never talking down but always pointing out what matters and why. It reads like a polished lecture transcript: formal enough for a textbook, conversational enough to hold a student's attention.

## Tone

Conversational-academic hybrid. The dominant register is semi-formal: complete sentences, precise terminology, correct grammar — but peppered with direct reader address, occasional contractions, rhetorical questions, and parenthetical asides that let personality show through. The tone conveys quiet confidence: the authors know the material deeply and present it as something the reader *can* and *will* learn, given effort. There is no pomposity, no opacity for its own sake, and no false casualness.

## Sentence Structure

- **Average length**: Medium to long (20-35 words in expository passages).
- **Variation pattern**: Long, clause-rich sentences build the argument; short punchy sentences deliver emphasis. The rhythm is build-build-build-punch. A paragraph might have four complex sentences followed by a two-word sentence that lands the point.
- **Voice**: Active voice dominates. "We observe..." not "It is observed that..." The passive appears mainly in formal definitions and established mathematical conventions.
- **Complexity**: Sentences frequently contain subordinate clauses, but each clause adds genuine information. Multi-clause sentences are structured so the main point comes early and qualifications follow.

## Paragraph Structure

- **Typical length**: 4-8 sentences in expository passages; 2-4 sentences in proof bodies.
- **Density**: Moderate. Each paragraph has one clear purpose — introduce a concept, work through an example, or make an observation.
- **Topic sentence pattern**: The first sentence usually states or signals the paragraph's topic. The remaining sentences develop it through explanation, example, or argumentation.
- **Transitions between paragraphs**: Typically the first word or phrase of a new paragraph connects it to the previous one.

## Vocabulary

- **Level**: Accessible. The text assumes mathematical maturity at the calculus level but defines every technical term explicitly on first use.
- **Jargon handling**: Every new term is bolded at its first occurrence and given a precise, formal definition. Even terms the reader likely knows (e.g., "even," "odd") are defined before being used in proofs.
- **Register**: Semi-formal. Words like "indeed," "consequently," and "to be sure" are common, but so are "let's," "you've," and "isn't."
- **Deliberate redundancy**: Key concepts are stated multiple ways — first informally, then formally, sometimes with a third restatement in the reader's likely vernacular.

## Transitions

- **Between sentences**: "Indeed,...", "Of course,...", "In fact,...", "Consequently,...", "Therefore,...", "Hence,...", "Thus,...", "On the other hand,...", "Observe that..."
- **Between paragraphs**: "We now turn to...", "Let's now consider...", "We are now prepared to...", "Let's look at..."
- **Between sections**: Forward references ("As we will see in Chapter X...") and backward references ("As we mentioned earlier..."). Sections often end with a statement that bridges to what comes next.
- **Characteristic pattern**: Signposting is explicit. The reader always knows where they are in the argument and where they're headed.

## Humor and Personality

- **Level**: Subtle but consistent.
- **Type**: Warm and literary. Cultural references (Disney's Pinocchio, Harry Potter, musical theater) appear alongside mathematical content. Quotes from famous writers, scientists, and mathematicians are woven in. Self-aware observations about the writing process itself surface occasionally.
- **Function**: Humor always serves the pedagogical purpose — it lightens a dense passage, makes a concept memorable, or humanizes the mathematical enterprise. It never distracts.

## Directness

- **Approach**: Build-then-reveal for new concepts; state-then-explain for known results.
- **Pattern**: Motivation and context come first, then informal intuition, then formal definitions, then examples, then theorems. The reader is led to understanding rather than confronted with it.
- **Hedging**: Minimal. When the authors are certain, they say so directly. Qualifications like "to be sure" and "it is important to note" are reserved for genuine caveats, not false modesty.
- **Emphasis**: Italics are used liberally for emphasis. Key points may be stated, then restated with force: "In mathematics writing is important, *very* important."

## Emotional Warmth

- **Reader relationship**: Mentor-to-promising-student. The authors believe in the reader's potential and say so. They acknowledge that the material is challenging but frame difficulty as surmountable with effort.
- **Encouragement**: Explicit and sincere. "This is an attainable goal if you have the desire." The authors push back against defeatist attitudes: "That's only an alibi."
- **Acknowledgment of struggle**: Honest. They don't pretend everything is easy. "If you run into snags (and you will), talk them over with a classmate or your instructor."
- **Celebration**: Small victories are acknowledged. When a proof technique works elegantly, the text pauses to appreciate it.

## Explanation Structure

The recurring pedagogical pattern (the "Chartrand sequence") is:

1. **Contextual motivation**: Why does this topic matter? Where does it fit in the mathematical landscape? Connect to the reader's likely prior experience.
2. **Informal intuition**: Describe the concept in everyday language, using familiar analogies or relatable scenarios.
3. **Formal definition**: State the precise mathematical definition. Bold the term being defined.
4. **Concrete examples**: Present labeled examples (Example X.Y) with detailed solutions that walk through reasoning step by step.
5. **Theorems/Results**: State the formal result. Use "Result" for most statements; reserve "Theorem" for especially significant or frequently-referenced ones.
6. **Proofs**: Present the proof, using inclusive "we" voice.
7. **Proof Analysis or commentary**: Reflect on what the proof did, why it works, what choices were made, and what could have been done differently.

Not every section follows all seven steps, but the general flow — motivation before formalism, examples before theorems, reflection after proofs — is highly consistent.

### Adaptive Scaffolding

Scaffolding intensity scales with the difficulty of the material. For concrete, accessible topics, the sequence is streamlined: definition, examples, result, proof. For abstract or conceptually difficult material (e.g., epsilon-delta proofs), a more elaborate triple structure appears:

1. **Proof Strategy** (scratch work): An informal, exploratory section showing how the proof is *constructed* — what choices are made and why. Uses phrases like "This suggests our choice of delta."
2. **Proof** (formal): The polished, complete proof in standard mathematical voice.
3. **Proof Analysis** (reflection): Unpacks the proof, addresses questions the reader might have ("How did we know to choose epsilon = 1?"), and validates that the real work happened in the strategy phase.

This metacognitive approach explicitly teaches proof *construction*, not just finished proofs.

### The Contrastive Teaching Pattern

When teaching conventions or correcting common errors, the authors use a distinctive incorrect-then-correct structure: show the wrong way first, explain why it's wrong, then show the right way. "Instead of writing [X]... it is preferable to write [Y]." This pattern respects the reader's intelligence by explaining *why* the convention matters, not just asserting it.

### Interior Monologue

Occasionally, the authors adopt a student's reasoning voice to make abstract thinking feel embodied — narrating what someone might think through a problem. This technique appears especially in strategy sections and puzzle-style problems.

## Openings

Chapters open with a contextualizing paragraph that places the topic within the larger mathematical landscape. The opening often references the reader's likely prior experience or connects to everyday life before pivoting to the mathematical content. The first word of each chapter is rendered with a decorative drop capital.

Section openings tend to begin with a bridging sentence that connects to the previous section or to the chapter's overall theme.

## Endings

Sections end with exercise sets. The final expository paragraph before exercises often makes a forward reference to upcoming material or delivers a summarizing observation about the section's key takeaway. Chapter endings typically include "Supplemental Exercises" that integrate concepts from multiple sections.

## Punctuation Habits

- **Parenthetical asides**: Extremely common. Used for clarifications, minor qualifications, and gentle editorial commentary. Often contain complete sentences.
- **Semicolons**: Frequent. Used to connect related independent clauses, especially when the second clause extends or qualifies the first.
- **Colons**: Introduce formal definitions, lists, and explanatory expansions.
- **Italics**: Heavy use for emphasis, for labels (Proof, Solution), and for quoted material.
- **Bold**: Reserved exclusively for newly defined technical terms.
- **Em-dashes**: Rare. Parentheses are preferred for asides.

## Common Rhetorical Moves

- **"Indeed,..."**: Introduces a supporting fact or strengthening observation.
- **"Of course,..."**: Signals something the reader should already know or that follows naturally.
- **"To be sure,..."**: Introduces a qualification or nuance.
- **"Observe that..." / "First, observe that..."**: Directs attention to a key step in reasoning.
- **"It follows that..."**: Marks a logical consequence.
- **"Consequently,..." / "Therefore,..." / "Hence,..."**: Marks deductive conclusions with varying degrees of formality.
- **"Let's consider..." / "Let's look at..."**: Invites the reader into a shared investigation.
- **"We are now prepared to..."**: Signals a milestone — enough groundwork has been laid to tackle the main event.
- **Rhetorical questions**: Used to motivate new topics. "But why would we want to discover new mathematics?"
- **Historical/biographical context**: Famous problems are introduced with their human stories (Fermat's marginal note, Guthrie's map coloring, Goldbach's correspondence).
- **"To see why this is so..."**: Introduces informal justifications with step-by-step reasoning.
- **"Since [premise], it follows that [conclusion]"**: The dominant logical inference structure within proofs.
- **Contrastive correction**: "Instead of writing [X]... it is preferable to write [Y]" — shows the wrong way, then the right way.
- **Motivating limitations**: Before introducing a new proof technique, showing where the current technique fails.

## Things to Avoid

- **Dry, impersonal prose**: Never eliminate the human presence from the writing.
- **Undefined jargon**: Never use a technical term without defining it first.
- **Skipping motivation**: Never jump straight to a definition without contextualizing why it matters.
- **"Obviously" or "clearly"**: These words dismiss the reader's potential confusion and should be used only when the claim truly is obvious at the reader's level.
- **Bullet-point lists in place of prose**: The style flows in paragraphs, not lists (except in exercises).
- **Condescension**: Maintain the balance between clarity and respect for the reader's intelligence.
- **Rushing**: The pacing is deliberate. Each concept gets the space it needs.
- **Disconnection from human curiosity**: Mathematics is always presented as a human enterprise, not a cold abstraction.
- **Excessive formalism**: Symbols and notation supplement words; they never replace clear prose explanation.
- **Passive voice in exposition**: "We prove..." not "It is proved that..."

## Examples: Neutral Text Rewritten in This Style

### Original (neutral)
> A function is injective if different inputs always produce different outputs.

### In this style
> We now turn to a property of functions that plays an important role in many areas of mathematics. A function *f* from a set *A* to a set *B* is called **one-to-one** (or **injective**) if whenever *a*₁ and *a*₂ are elements of *A* with *a*₁ ≠ *a*₂, then *f*(*a*₁) ≠ *f*(*a*₂). In other words, different elements of *A* are mapped to different elements of *B* by *f*. Let's look at a few examples to see what this means in practice.

---

### Original (neutral)
> The Pigeonhole Principle: if n items are placed into m containers where n > m, then at least one container holds more than one item.

### In this style
> We now describe a principle that, despite its simplicity, has remarkable power. Perhaps you have noticed that if five letters are placed in four mailboxes, then some mailbox must contain at least two letters. This is an instance of what is known as the **Pigeonhole Principle**. While the idea itself may seem straightforward (and, indeed, it is), its applications are often quite surprising, as we will see.

---

### Original (neutral)
> Before starting the proof, note that this technique requires assuming the hypothesis is true and deriving the conclusion.

### In this style
> We are now prepared to illustrate the direct proof technique. Recall that in a direct proof of *P*(*x*) ⇒ *Q*(*x*), we assume that *P*(*x*) is true for an arbitrary element *x* and then show that *Q*(*x*) must be true for this same element. To be sure, this is the technique that occurs more often in mathematics than any other. We follow the proof by a proof analysis.
