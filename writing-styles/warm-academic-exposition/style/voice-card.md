# Voice Card: Warm Academic Exposition

> One-page quick reference for writing in this voice.

## In a Nutshell

A patient mathematics professor at the whiteboard: authoritative but warm, rigorous but accessible. Uses inclusive "we" to walk alongside the reader. Defines every term, motivates every concept, and reflects on every proof. Reads like a polished lecture — formal enough for a textbook, human enough to hold attention.

## Key Traits

- **Tone**: Conversational-academic — semi-formal with personality showing through
- **Formality**: Complete sentences, precise terms, but occasional contractions and rhetorical questions
- **Warmth**: Mentor-to-promising-student — believes in the reader, acknowledges difficulty honestly
- **Pacing**: Deliberate — motivation before formalism, examples before theorems, analysis after proofs
- **Reader address**: Inclusive "we" dominates; "you" for direct advice; "let's" for transitions

## The Pattern

1. Motivate: why does this matter? Connect to what the reader already knows
2. Intuit: describe informally in plain language
3. Define: state precisely, bold the new term
4. Exemplify: labeled examples with step-by-step solutions
5. Formalize: state and prove theorems
6. Reflect: analyze the proof — what worked, what choices were made

## Signature Moves

- "We are now prepared to..." (milestone signpost)
- "Indeed,..." / "Of course,..." / "To be sure,..." (connecting observations)
- Parenthetical asides for gentle clarifications and commentary
- Historical and cultural references to humanize the mathematics
- Short punchy sentences after long buildups for emphasis
- Italics for emphasis; bold only for defined terms
- Contrastive correction: show wrong way, explain why, show right way
- For hard material: Proof Strategy (scratch work) → Proof → Proof Analysis (reflection)
- Scale scaffolding to difficulty — more metacognitive narration when concepts are harder

## Never Do This

- Skip the motivation — never jump straight to a definition
- Use undefined jargon — every technical term gets a formal definition at first use
- Say "obviously" or "clearly" when it isn't to a learner
- Write in passive voice for exposition — use active "we"
- Present math as cold abstraction disconnected from human curiosity

## Quick Test

Read your draft aloud. It should sound like a professor who genuinely cares whether the student understands — someone who would stay after class to explain again, but would also gently push back if the student said "I'm just not a math person."
